% ABSTRACT
% ============================================================================
\begin{abstract}
The Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT) and Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT) are the default sparse bases for natural images, chosen long ago for mathematical tractability rather than proven optimality.
We ask whether a compact parametric circuit, obtained by relaxing the Hadamard and controlled-phase gates of the Cooley--Tukey FFT into free elements of $U(2)$ and $U(1)^4$, can discover a sparser basis by end-to-end Riemannian optimization on the unitary manifold.
On DIV2K natural images at $256 \times 256$, a block-wrapped variant of the learned circuit (Blocked RealRichBasis) beats full-image DCT by $+2.85$~dB PSNR at $20\%$ coefficient retention and essentially matches JPEG's $8 \times 8$ BlockDCT ($-0.3$~dB), showing that learned unitary bases can ride right up to the hand-designed reference once an explicit block prior is introduced.
Inspecting the trained gate parameters gives a mechanistic reading: training spontaneously rediscovers block locality, the inductive bias hand-designed into JPEG, without any block prior in the loss.
The framework is released as an open-source Julia library, \texttt{ParametricDFT.jl}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Every major image codec, from JPEG~\cite{wallace1992jpeg} to HEVC~\cite{sullivan2012hevc}, is built on the same principle: project the image onto a fixed unitary basis, keep the largest coefficients, discard the rest~\cite{goyal2001transform, mallat2008wavelet}.
Reconstruction quality is set almost entirely by how \emph{sparsely} the chosen basis represents natural images, which makes the basis the single most consequential design choice in the codec pipeline.
In practice that choice has remained narrow: the Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT)~\cite{cooley1965fft}, the Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT)~\cite{ahmed1974dct}, and their block-wise variants have dominated every deployed image and video codec for decades.

A parallel literature, originating far from image coding, has built up a very different language for describing linear transforms on high-dimensional arrays.
Tensor networks were introduced as a compact description of quantum many-body states, with hierarchical constructions such as MERA~\cite{vidal2007mera} and time-evolution protocols such as TEBD~\cite{vidal2004tebd}, and have since been adopted as machine-learning models in their own right: matrix-product and tree-tensor networks have been used for supervised classification~\cite{stoudenmire2016supervised}, generative modeling~\cite{han2018unsupervised}, and the compression of high-dimensional arrays via tensor-train decomposition~\cite{oseledets2011tensor}, aided by differentiable contraction pipelines on GPU that have matured only recently~\cite{liu2021tropical, liu2023generic, OMEinsum}.
Parameterized quantum circuits emerged in parallel as the workhorse of variational quantum algorithms~\cite{mcclean2016variational, cerezo2021variational} and quantum machine learning~\cite{schuld2019quantum, benedetti2019parameterized}, offering a disciplined way to search over unitary transforms, though with well-documented optimization challenges such as barren plateaus~\cite{mcclean2018barren, cerezo2021cost}.
Images specifically have been studied through quantum representations such as FRQI~\cite{le2011frqi} and NEQR~\cite{zhang2013neqr}, which encode pixel intensities into the amplitudes of a quantum state, and through tensor-network simulation of the resulting circuits~\cite{markov2008simulating, luo2020yao}; these constructions treat the quantum circuit as a \emph{representation} of a fixed transform rather than as a space to be searched.
The observation that closes this circle is that the DFT itself is famously a quantum circuit, the Quantum Fourier Transform, whose Hadamard and controlled-phase gates exactly implement the Cooley--Tukey butterfly.
If the DFT is a quantum circuit, relaxing its gates into free unitaries turns the basis-selection problem into a search over a compact parametric circuit.

The reason this program has not already been carried out is twofold.
On one side, no principled, low-dimensional, unitary basis search exists that strictly outperforms the DCT while remaining inspectable: modern end-to-end learned codecs~\cite{balle2017end} sidestep the question by discarding the fixed-basis picture entirely and absorbing everything into a convolutional autoencoder, and block-transform learning~\cite{pons2024rdlt} parametrizes the transform in unconstrained Euclidean space and fixes the block size, trading away the unitarity that underwrites the transform-coding guarantee.
On the other side, a direct gradient-based search over unitary bases runs into two compounding obstacles: unitarity is a hard manifold constraint that a Euclidean gradient step immediately violates, which is precisely the setting in which Riemannian optimization on matrix manifolds~\cite{edelman1998geometry, absil2008optimization, boumal2023introduction, becigneul2019riemannian, kasai2019riemannian} and on the unitary group in particular~\cite{khaneja2005optimal, wen2013feasible} is the natural tool; and the top-$k$ coefficient selection that defines transform coding is non-differentiable, so gradients from the reconstruction loss do not reach the transform parameters through the obvious chain rule~\cite{bengio2013estimating}.
Either obstacle alone is manageable; together they are why a naive Euclidean parametrization of a unitary, trained under the MSE reconstruction loss, typically fails to match even the full-image DCT.

We resolve both obstacles by choosing the parametrization and the optimizer jointly.
For the parametrization, we take the Cooley--Tukey FFT tensor network and relax each Hadamard gate into a free element of $U(2)$ and each controlled-phase into a free element of $U(1)^4$, giving a compact parametric circuit that contains the DFT as a single point, uses only $O(\log^2 N)$ parameters in the image side $N$, and is unitary by construction rather than by penalty.
For the optimizer, we search its parameter manifold $U(2) \times U(1)^4$ with Riemannian Adam~\cite{kingma2015adam, becigneul2019riemannian} using Cayley retractions~\cite{wen2013feasible, li2020efficient} that preserve unitarity exactly at any step size and parallel transport of momentum between tangent spaces; top-$k$ non-differentiability is handled by a straight-through gradient estimator~\cite{bengio2013estimating}, and the whole pipeline runs as a batched einsum contraction under automatic differentiation~\cite{innes2018dont, OMEinsum, luo2020yao, liao2019differentiable}.
Four contributions follow.
(i)~We show that the DFT and DCT are \emph{not} optimal sparse bases for natural images: a learned circuit in our framework strictly outperforms the full-image DCT at every retention rate we test on DIV2K~\cite{div2k} and Quick Draw~\cite{quickdraw}.
(ii)~We give a gate-level mechanistic reading of the learned basis, showing that training spontaneously rediscovers block locality, the inductive bias JPEG hand-codes, without any block structure built into the loss.
(iii)~Within the framework we compare four circuit topologies (separable QFT, an Entangled QFT with cross-dimensional coupling, TEBD-style ring connectivity~\cite{vidal2004tebd}, and a MERA-inspired hierarchical circuit~\cite{vidal2007mera}) and find a stable ranking that identifies cross-dimensional all-to-all coupling as the decisive inductive bias.
(iv)~We release \texttt{ParametricDFT.jl}, an open-source Julia implementation combining tensor network contraction, Riemannian optimization, and differentiable programming.

The rest of the paper proceeds as follows.
\Cref{sec:background} derives the parametric circuit from the Cooley--Tukey FFT.
\Cref{sec:circuits} defines the four circuit topologies, \cref{sec:training} the Riemannian search, and \cref{sec:experiments} reports the main results and the gate-level analysis (\cref{sec:emergence}).
\Cref{sec:discussion} discusses implications.

\section{From Cooley--Tukey FFT to a Parametric Circuit}
\label{sec:background}

\begin{figure*}[!tbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{cooley_tukey_to_qft.pdf}
  \caption{Four transformations that turn the Cooley--Tukey FFT into a parametric circuit, drawn for $k = 4$ qubits ($N = 16$). (a)~\emph{Re-index}: the length-$N$ vector is read as a rank-$k$ tensor with one binary leg per qubit. (b)~\emph{Recurse}: one Cooley--Tukey level factors $F_n$ into a half-size DFT, a block-diagonal conditional twiddle, and an outer Hadamard on $q_0$. (c)~\emph{Decompose}: the conditional twiddle becomes a chain of $k{-}1$ controlled-phase gates $M_j$ coupling $q_0$ to each lower-significance qubit. (d)~\emph{Relax}: unrolling the recursion yields a circuit of $k$ Hadamards and $k(k-1)/2$ controlled-phase gates; relaxing each gate into a free element of $U(2)$ or $U(1)^4$ produces the parametric circuit trained in \cref{sec:circuits,sec:training}.}
  \label{fig:cooley_tukey_to_qft}
\end{figure*}

This section derives the parametric circuit that the rest of the paper trains.
Starting from the Cooley--Tukey FFT, we apply four local transformations to the underlying tensor network---\emph{re-index}, \emph{recurse}, \emph{decompose}, and \emph{relax}---ending with a circuit whose gates are free elements of the unitary manifold.
The construction is implemented in the open-source Julia library \texttt{ParametricDFT.jl}, whose \texttt{note/main.typ} gives the same derivation in executable form; \cref{fig:cooley_tukey_to_qft} shows the four transformations side by side for a $k = 4$-qubit ($N = 16$) example.

\subsection{Cooley--Tukey decomposition}

The DFT matrix of size $n = 2^k$ has entries $(F_n)_{i,j} = \omega^{(i-1)(j-1)}$ with $\omega = e^{-2\pi i / n}$ the primitive $n$-th root of unity, so that $F_n$ is the Vandermonde matrix
\begin{equation}
  F_n = \begin{pmatrix}
    1 & 1 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \\
    1 & \omega & \omega^2 & \cdots & \omega^{n-1} \\
    1 & \omega^2 & \omega^4 & \cdots & \omega^{2(n-1)} \\
    \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
    1 & \omega^{n-1} & \omega^{2(n-1)} & \cdots & \omega^{(n-1)^2}
  \end{pmatrix}.
  \label{eq:vandermonde}
\end{equation}
Split the columns by parity (odd $j = 1, 3, \ldots$; even $j = 2, 4, \ldots$) and the rows at $n/2$. Each of the four resulting $(n/2) \times (n/2)$ blocks reduces to the half-size DFT up to the twiddle $D_{n/2} = \diag(1, \omega, \ldots, \omega^{n/2-1})$:
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}
    {[F_n]}^{\text{odd}}_{\text{top}} &= {[F_n]}^{\text{odd}}_{\text{bot}} = F_{n/2}, \\
    {[F_n]}^{\text{even}}_{\text{top}} &= -{[F_n]}^{\text{even}}_{\text{bot}} = D_{n/2}\, F_{n/2},
  \end{aligned}
  \label{eq:blocks}
\end{equation}
using $(\omega^2)^{(i-1)(j-1)} = (F_{n/2})_{i,j}$ for the odd columns, $\omega^n = 1$ for the bottom-odd block, and $\omega^{n/2} = -1$ for the bottom-even block.
Permuting $\mathbf{x}$ so that odd-indexed entries precede even-indexed entries and assembling the four blocks gives the Cooley--Tukey factorization
\begin{equation}
  F_n \mathbf{x}
  = \begin{pmatrix} I_{n/2} & D_{n/2} \\ I_{n/2} & -D_{n/2} \end{pmatrix}
    \begin{pmatrix} F_{n/2} & 0 \\ 0 & F_{n/2} \end{pmatrix}
    \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{x}_{\text{odd}} \\ \mathbf{x}_{\text{even}} \end{pmatrix}.
  \label{eq:fft}
\end{equation}
Applied recursively, each level reduces an $n$-point DFT to two $(n/2)$-point DFTs plus $O(n)$ twiddle multiplications, giving total cost $O(N \log N)$.
\Cref{eq:fft} is the input to the tensor-network construction.

\subsection{Building the parametric circuit}
\label{sec:gate_decomposition}

\emph{Re-index} (\cref{fig:cooley_tukey_to_qft}a). A vector of length $n = 2^k$ carries a single integer index that we write in binary as $i = q_0 + 2 q_1 + \cdots + 2^{k-1} q_{k-1}$.
Re-interpreting $\mathbf{x}$ as a rank-$k$ tensor with one binary leg $q_j$ per qubit is purely notational, but it turns every linear operator in~\cref{eq:fft} into a tensor-network component acting on specific qubit legs, which is what makes the gate decomposition below possible.

\emph{Recurse} (\cref{fig:cooley_tukey_to_qft}b). Take $q_0$ as the most-significant bit labelling the top and bottom row-blocks of~\cref{eq:fft}. The outer block matrix factors through the butterfly identity
\begin{equation}
  \begin{pmatrix} I_{n/2} & D_{n/2} \\ I_{n/2} & -D_{n/2} \end{pmatrix}
  = (H \otimes I_{n/2})
    \begin{pmatrix} I_{n/2} & 0 \\ 0 & D_{n/2} \end{pmatrix},
  \label{eq:butterfly-id}
\end{equation}
with $H = \bigl(\begin{smallmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$ the unnormalized Hadamard matrix. Substituting into~\cref{eq:fft} gives
\begin{equation}
  F_n \;=\; \bigl( H \otimes I_{n/2} \bigr) \,
            \begin{pmatrix} I_{n/2} & 0 \\ 0 & D_{n/2} \end{pmatrix} \,
            \bigl( I_2 \otimes F_{n/2} \bigr).
  \label{eq:butterfly}
\end{equation}
The rightmost factor applies $F_{n/2}$ to the sub-register $(q_1, \ldots, q_{k-1})$; the middle factor is a diagonal twiddle conditioned on $q_0$; the leftmost factor is a Hadamard acting on $q_0$ alone.

\emph{Decompose} (\cref{fig:cooley_tukey_to_qft}c). The conditional twiddle in the middle of~\cref{eq:butterfly} factors into two-qubit controlled-phase gates.
Using $D_n = \diag(1, \omega^{n/2}) \otimes \diag(1, \omega^{n/4}) \otimes \cdots \otimes \diag(1, \omega)$,
\begin{equation}
  \begin{pmatrix} I_{n/2} & 0 \\ 0 & D_{n/2} \end{pmatrix}
  \;=\; \prod_{j=1}^{k-1} \mathrm{CP}_{0,j}\bigl(M_j\bigr),
  \label{eq:cphase}
\end{equation}
with $M_j = \diag(1, 1, 1, e^{i\pi/2^{j-1}})$, where $\mathrm{CP}_{0,j}(M_j)$ applies $M_j$ to the qubit pair $(q_0, q_j)$ when both qubits are $\ket{1}$.
The conditional twiddle has become a chain of $k - 1$ two-qubit diagonal gates.

\emph{Relax} (\cref{fig:cooley_tukey_to_qft}d). Recursing on $F_{n/2}$ replays the recursion-and-decomposition pair on the sub-register $(q_1, \ldots, q_{k-1})$, and so on down to $F_1 = H$ on the final qubit.
The complete network has $k$ Hadamards (one per qubit) and $k(k-1)/2$ controlled-phase gates (one per ordered qubit pair); contracting it costs $O(N \log N)$ and reproduces the classical FFT.
This fixed circuit is the standard Quantum Fourier Transform.
To obtain a \emph{parametric} circuit we relax each gate into a free element of its unitary manifold: every Hadamard becomes an unconstrained element of $U(2)$ (four real parameters), and every $M_j$ becomes an unconstrained element of $\diag(1, 1, 1, e^{i\phi_j}) \in U(1)^4$ (one real parameter).
The resulting circuit has $4k + k(k-1)/2 = O(\log^2 N)$ real parameters in the image side $N = 2^k$, contains the classical DFT at the QFT parameter point, and is unitary at every parameter setting by construction.

\subsection{Two-dimensional transforms and parameter count}

For a 2D image of size $2^m \times 2^n$, the separable 2D DFT is $F_m \otimes F_n$.
We apply the 1D parametric circuit independently to the $m$-qubit row register and the $n$-qubit column register, giving $m + n$ Hadamards and $m(m-1)/2 + n(n-1)/2$ controlled-phase gates across both dimensions.
The parameter count remains $O(\log^2 N)$ in the image side $N = 2^m = 2^n$, and the circuit still initializes exactly at the classical 2D DFT.
\Cref{sec:circuits} introduces three variants of this separable construction that differ only in their gate connectivity: an Entangled QFT that couples corresponding row and column qubits, a TEBD-style ring, and a MERA-inspired hierarchical topology.

\section{Parametric Circuit Topologies}
\label{sec:circuits}

Each topology defines a parametric unitary transform $\TT(\boldsymbol{\theta}): \CC^{2^m \times 2^n} \to \CC^{2^m \times 2^n}$ over the same gate parameterization---Hadamard-like gates $H \in U(2)$ and controlled-phase gates $M = \diag(1, 1, 1, e^{i\phi}) \in U(1)^4$.
Setting all parameters to their QFT values recovers the classical DFT.
We organise the topologies into two subsections: the \emph{basic variants} (\cref{sec:basic_variants}) act on the full image as a single unitary, while the \emph{block-wrapped variants} (\cref{sec:block_wrapper}) tile the same circuits across $b \times b$ tiles of the image.

\subsection{Basic variants}
\label{sec:basic_variants}

The basic variants act on the full image as a single $2^m \times 2^n$ unitary; they differ only in how the controlled-phase chain is wired (\cref{tab:circuits}).
The \emph{separable QFT} (\cref{sec:qft_basis}) directly parameterizes the tensor network of \cref{sec:gate_decomposition}, with $m + n$ Hadamard gates on $U(2)$ and $m(m-1)/2 + n(n-1)/2$ controlled-phase gates on $U(1)^4$, giving the product transform $\TT = F_m(\boldsymbol{\theta}_{\text{row}}) \otimes F_n(\boldsymbol{\theta}_{\text{col}})$.
Setting all parameters to their QFT values recovers the classical DFT.

\paragraph{Entangled QFT.}
\label{sec:qft_basis}
The separable QFT processes row and column dimensions independently, ignoring spatial correlations between them.
Natural images carry strong cross-dimensional correlations (diagonal edges, radial textures), so we add $n$ entanglement gates $E_k = \diag(1, 1, 1, e^{i\phi_k})$ coupling the qubit pairs $(x_{n-k}, y_{n-k})$:
\begin{align}
  \TT_{\text{entangled}} &= U_{\text{entangle}} \cdot (F_n \otimes F_n), \nonumber \\
  U_{\text{entangle}} &= \prod_{k=1}^n E_k.
  \label{eq:entangled_qft}
\end{align}
The construction adds only $n$ parameters, keeps the per-dimension cost at $O(N \log N)$, and reduces to the separable QFT at $\phi_k = 0$ (\cref{fig:entangled_qft}).

\begin{figure}[!tbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{entangled_qft_circuit.pdf}

Row qubits $x_k$ and column qubits $y_k$ each pass through independent QFT circuits (Hadamard gates $H$ and controlled-phase gates $M_j$). \paragraph{TEBD and MERA controls.}
\label{sec:alt_topologies}
To stress-test the claim that cross-dimensional, all-to-all coupling is the decisive structural ingredient, we add two connectivity foils: a TEBD-style~\cite{vidal2004tebd} \emph{ring} (nearest-neighbor controlled-phase gates with wrap-around, $O(n)$ depth, $2n$ phase parameters total) and a MERA-inspired~\cite{vidal2007mera} \emph{hierarchical} circuit (disentanglers and isometries at increasing strides, $O(\log n)$ depth, $4(n-1)$ phase parameters).
Both share the same $U(2)$ Hadamard layer as the QFT but replace its all-to-all phase chain with local or multi-scale connectivity.
Their gate-level construction and circuit diagrams are deferred to \cref{app:alt_topologies}; we use them purely as controls in the topology comparison of \cref{sec:topology_div2k}.

\subsection{Block-wrapped variants}
\label{sec:block_wrapper}

The four topologies above act on the full image as a single $2^m \times 2^n$ unitary.
A second design axis, motivated directly by the block-DCT used in JPEG~\cite{wallace1992jpeg}, wraps any of these inner circuits in an explicit block partition: the image is reshaped into a grid of $b \times b$ tiles and the same parametric circuit $U(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ is applied independently to each tile,
\begin{equation}
  \TT_{\text{block}}(\boldsymbol{\theta})(\mathbf{x})_{[i,j]}
  = U(\boldsymbol{\theta})\, \mathbf{x}_{[i,j]},
  \quad \mathbf{x}_{[i,j]} \in \CC^{b \times b},
  \label{eq:block_wrapper}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{x}_{[i,j]}$ is the $(i,j)$-th block.
The wrapper is generic over the inner topology, so each of the four circuits in \cref{tab:circuits} has a blocked counterpart (Blocked QFT, Blocked Ent.\ QFT, Blocked TEBD, Blocked MERA).
\Cref{fig:block_qft_circuit} shows the construction at the image level; \cref{fig:block_basis_circuits} sketches the inner-circuit variants used in the pdft-benchmarks harness (the standard QFT-style $H$~$+$~CP topology, RichBasis with $H + U(4)$ two-qubit gates, and RealRichBasis with $H + O(4)$).

The block size $b$ is the structural prior: $b = N$ recovers the full-image circuits already defined; $b = 8$ matches the JPEG block grid.
The unblocked separable QFT, when trained on natural images, in fact \emph{discovers} a block structure on its own---several Hadamard-role gates collapse to near-Pauli matrices at convergence, freezing the corresponding qubits into classical block indices; this is shown in detail in \cref{sec:emergence}.
The emergent block structure is one motivation for offering an explicit block prior as a complementary, hand-designed structural choice; the experiments in \cref{sec:topology_div2k,sec:blockprior_results} report results for both.

\section{Training Objective and Optimization}
\label{sec:training}

\subsection{Loss functions}

We train all reported results under the MSE reconstruction loss
\begin{equation}
  \LL_{\text{MSE}}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \|\mathbf{x} - \TT(\boldsymbol{\theta})^{-1}(\topk(\TT(\boldsymbol{\theta})(\mathbf{x}), k))\|_F^2,
  \label{eq:mse_loss}
\end{equation}
where $\topk(\cdot, k)$ retains the $k$ coefficients with the highest frequency-weighted score (\cref{sec:truncation}) and zeros out the rest.
Unlike the two natural alternatives---the $\ell_1$ norm $\LL_{L_1} = \sum_{i,j} |\TT(\mathbf{x})_{i,j}|$, the tightest convex relaxation of $\ell_0$ (sparsity promoting), and the $\ell_2$ norm $\LL_{L_2} = \sum_{i,j}|\TT(\mathbf{x})_{i,j}|^2$ (energy concentrating)---the MSE loss in~\cref{eq:mse_loss} optimizes the end-to-end reconstruction pipeline \emph{including} truncation, so gradients from the truncation layer (\cref{sec:topk_grad}) reach the transform parameters directly.
We found in preliminary experiments that the MSE objective both trains more stably and yields better rate-distortion than $L_1$ or $L_2$, consistent with the observation that $L_1$ and $L_2$ are only \emph{proxies} for the downstream reconstruction quality that MSE measures directly.

\subsection{Frequency-weighted truncation}
\label{sec:truncation}

The top-$k$ selection uses a frequency-weighted score rather than raw magnitude:
\begin{equation}
  s_{i,j} = |y_{i,j}| \cdot (1 + w_{i,j}),
  \quad
  w_{i,j} = 1 - \frac{d_{i,j}}{2\, d_{\max}},
\end{equation}
where $d_{i,j} = \sqrt{(i - c_i)^2 + (j - c_j)^2}$ is the distance from frequency bin $(i,j)$ to the DC component and $d_{\max}$ is the maximum such distance.
Low-frequency coefficients receive up to a $2\times$ boost in their retention score, consistent with the human visual system's greater sensitivity to low-frequency content.

\subsection{Straight-through gradient for top-$k$}
\label{sec:topk_grad}

The top-$k$ truncation is non-differentiable: the set of retained indices changes discontinuously.
We define a custom chain rule that passes gradients through retained coefficients and zeros out the rest---the \emph{straight-through estimator}~\cite{bengio2013estimating} applied to truncation:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial \topk(\mathbf{y}, k)}{\partial y_{i,j}} =
  \begin{cases}
    1 & \text{if } (i,j) \in \mathcal{S}, \\
    0 & \text{otherwise},
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{S}$ is the set of retained indices.
This is justified because (i) the true Jacobian has delta-function contributions at the boundaries that are unusable for gradient-based optimization, (ii) the mask is locally stable during training, and (iii) the gradient correctly conveys ``adjust the retained coefficients to better approximate the signal.''
Alternative approaches to differentiating through discrete selections include the Gumbel-softmax reparameterization~\cite{jang2017categorical} and continuous relaxations of subset sampling~\cite{xie2019reparameterizable}; we find the straight-through estimator simpler to implement and sufficient in this setting because the retained mask $\mathcal{S}$ is locally stable along the optimization trajectory.

\subsection{Riemannian optimization}
\label{sec:riemannian}

The circuit parameters live on two manifolds whose geometry is developed in the standard references on matrix-manifold optimization~\cite{edelman1998geometry, absil2008optimization, boumal2023introduction, zhang2016first}:

\begin{definition}[Unitary manifold $U(2)$]
The group of $2 \times 2$ unitary matrices with tangent space $T_U U(2) = \{US : S^\dagger = -S\}$.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Phase manifold $U(1)^4$]
The product of four unit circles with tangent space $T_{\mathbf{z}} U(1)^4 = \{i\boldsymbol{\theta} \circ \mathbf{z} : \boldsymbol{\theta} \in \RR^4\}$.
\end{definition}

Given the Euclidean gradient $\nabla_E f$ from automatic differentiation:
\begin{align}
  \text{For } U(2): & \quad \grad f(U) = U \cdot \skew(U^\dagger \nabla_E f), \label{eq:grad_u2} \\
  \text{For } U(1)^4: & \quad \grad f(\mathbf{z}) = i \cdot \operatorname{Im}(\bar{\mathbf{z}} \circ \nabla_E f) \circ \mathbf{z}, \label{eq:grad_u1}
\end{align}
where $\skew(A) = (A - A^\dagger)/2$ extracts the skew-Hermitian part.

For $U(2)$, we use the Cayley retraction~\cite{wen2013feasible, li2020efficient}:
\begin{equation}
  R_U(\alpha \xi) = \left(I - \frac{\alpha}{2} W\right)^{-1} \left(I + \frac{\alpha}{2} W\right) U,
  \label{eq:cayley}
\end{equation}
where $W = \xi U^\dagger$ projected to $\mathfrak{u}(2)$ via $W \leftarrow (W - W^\dagger)/2$.
The Cayley map preserves unitarity exactly for any step size $\alpha$.
For $U(1)^4$, we use normalization: $R_{\mathbf{z}}(\alpha \xi) = (\mathbf{z} + \alpha \xi) / |\mathbf{z} + \alpha \xi|$ elementwise.

The update rule is $x_{t+1} = R_{x_t}(-\eta \cdot \grad f(x_t))$, where $\eta$ is determined by Armijo backtracking: starting from $\alpha_0$, contract $\alpha \leftarrow \tau \alpha$ (with $\tau = 0.5$) until the sufficient decrease condition is satisfied:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
  \LL\big(R_{x_t}&(-\alpha \grad f(x_t))\big) \\
  &\leq \LL(x_t) - c\, \alpha\, \|\grad f(x_t)\|^2,
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
with $c = 10^{-4}$.

We also implement Riemannian Adam~\cite{kingma2015adam, becigneul2019riemannian, kasai2019riemannian}, which maintains exponentially weighted first and second moment estimates of the Riemannian gradient with parallel transport of momentum between iterates.
Note that parameter-shift rules developed for hardware-quantum gradients~\cite{mitarai2018quantum, schuld2019evaluating} and the quantum natural gradient~\cite{stokes2020quantum} are unnecessary in our classical simulation setting, where Euclidean gradients from reverse-mode automatic differentiation through the einsum contraction are available essentially for free.
The bias-corrected update direction is $d_t = \hat{m}_t / (\sqrt{\hat{v}_t} + \epsilon)$, with $\beta_1 = 0.9$, $\beta_2 = 0.999$, $\epsilon = 10^{-8}$.
Momentum is transported between tangent spaces via projection:
\begin{equation}
  \Gamma_{x_t \to x_{t+1}}(m_t) = \proj_{T_{x_{t+1}} \MM}(m_t).
\end{equation}

\subsection{Batched einsum evaluation}

The forward transform is implemented as an Einstein summation (einsum) contraction of the image tensor with the gate tensors, following the differentiable tensor-network tradition~\cite{liao2019differentiable} and evaluated with Julia's reverse-mode automatic differentiation~\cite{innes2018dont}.
The contraction order is pre-optimized using a tree search algorithm (TreeSA) that minimizes the total operation count.

For batched training, a batch index $\beta$ is appended to the image input/output indices:
\begin{equation}
  Y_{q'_1, \ldots, q'_{m+n}, \beta} = \sum_{q_1, \ldots, q_{m+n}} \left(\prod_j G^j_{\cdots}\right) X_{q_1, \ldots, q_{m+n}, \beta}.
\end{equation}
The gate tensors are shared across the batch, reducing GPU kernel launch overhead from $O(B)$ to $O(1)$.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

We evaluate on two datasets:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Quick Draw}~\cite{quickdraw}: Hand-drawn sketches ($28 \times 28$, zero-padded to $32 \times 32$, i.e., $m = n = 5$ qubits). A small-scale sanity check.
  \item \textbf{DIV2K}~\cite{div2k}: High-resolution natural photographs, preprocessed to $256 \times 256$ ($m = n = 8$ qubits). This is the main benchmark; reported QFT results use a generalized training run over 800 training images.
\end{itemize}

All learned bases are trained using Riemannian gradient descent with Armijo line search ($\alpha_0 = 0.01$, $c = 10^{-4}$, $\tau = 0.5$, at most 10 backtracking steps), under the MSE reconstruction loss $\LL_{\text{MSE}}$ (\cref{eq:mse_loss}) with $k = 10\%$ coefficients retained during training.
We use a 20\% validation split, early stopping with patience 2 epochs, and random seed 42 throughout.

We report PSNR (peak signal-to-noise ratio, $10 \log_{10}(1/\text{MSE})$) and SSIM (structural similarity) at compression ratios $k/N \in \{5\%, 10\%, 15\%, 20\%\}$, averaged over the evaluation set.

We compare against three fixed classical transforms at different positions on the \emph{block-size axis}:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{FFT} --- the full-image DFT; the un-learned initialization of every QFT-family basis. Block size $=$ the full image.
  \item \textbf{DCT} --- the full-image Discrete Cosine Transform.
  \item \textbf{BlockDCT ($8 \times 8$)} --- the JPEG~\cite{wallace1992jpeg} block-DCT at its standard block size. Block size $=$ $8 \times 8$.
\end{itemize}
These three span the extremes along which block size varies; the learned QFT lies \emph{between} them in architecture, and the experiments show that it also lies between them in performance.

\subsection{The block-size axis: QFT, DCT and BlockDCT}
\label{sec:blocksize}

\cref{tab:div2k_repr} is the central result of this paper.

Three effects are visible at 20\% keep (the column that matters most for practical compression):
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \emph{Data-adaptive orientation gives $+1.5$~dB.} Full-image DCT (30.85~dB) beats full-image FFT (29.39~dB) by $+1.5$~dB because its real-valued cosine basis aligns better with natural-image statistics than the DFT's complex basis, while the block size is the same.

\subsection{Emergent block structure in the trained QFT}
\label{sec:emergence}

The learned QFT on $256 \times 256$ DIV2K images discovers an effective $16 \times 16$ block structure: of the 16 Hadamard-role gates in the trained circuit (8 per dimension), 7 collapse to near-Pauli-$Z$ or near-Pauli-$X$ matrices during training.
These ``frozen'' qubits act as classical block indices, factoring the transform into blocks of side $2^{4} = 16$ pixels on each axis.

Each $H$-role gate is a $2 \times 2$ unitary initialized as the Hadamard matrix $H = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1\end{pmatrix}$ and optimized over $U(2)$.
We classify a trained gate as
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Hadamard} ($H$) if all four of its entries have modulus $\approx 1/\sqrt{2}$,
  \item \emph{$Z$-like} if its diagonal entries have modulus $\approx 1$ and its off-diagonal entries have modulus $\approx 0$ (i.e., the gate is near-diagonal, behaving like a Pauli-$Z$ up to overall phase),
  \item \emph{$X$-like} if its off-diagonal entries have modulus $\approx 1$ and its diagonal entries have modulus $\approx 0$,
  \item and \emph{other} otherwise.
\end{itemize}
A proper Hadamard creates superposition across the $|0\rangle/|1\rangle$ states of its qubit, which is precisely the DFT butterfly operation that mixes frequencies along that axis.
A $Z$-like or $X$-like gate does \emph{not} create superposition: the computational-basis amplitudes are preserved (with possible sign or bit flips), and that qubit no longer participates in the DFT mixing.
Consequently, every qubit whose Hadamard-role gate has frozen to $Z$-like or $X$-like behaves as a \emph{classical block index}, and the transform factors into blocks of size $2^{m_{\mathrm{free}}} \times 2^{n_{\mathrm{free}}}$ pixels, where $m_{\mathrm{free}}$ and $n_{\mathrm{free}}$ are the number of still-mixing qubits in each dimension.

\Cref{fig:hadamard_freezing} shows the freezing pattern schematically and \cref{tab:gate_summary} summarizes the trained gate types.
In both dimensions, qubits 0--3 retain their Hadamard role and qubits 4--7 freeze to $Z$-like (with a single $X$-like in the column dimension).
The freezing is \emph{not} a smooth decay: the off-diagonal magnitudes of the frozen gates sit at the $\sim\!1\%$ level, essentially quantized to the $Z$-like or $X$-like endpoints.
Meanwhile all 56 controlled-phase gates retain their full $U(1)^4$ parameterization (none have collapsed to the identity), so the \emph{intra-block} transform remains rich.

This produces a clean mechanical reading of the block-size axis in~\cref{tab:div2k_repr}:
BlockDCT sits at block size $2^{3} = 8$ by hand design (each dimension has 3 frozen bits by construction);
the trained QFT sits at block size $2^{4} = 16$ because training has frozen 4 bits per dimension;
full-image DCT sits at block size $2^{8} = 256$ (no frozen bits).
The remaining $\sim\!1.5$~dB gap between BlockDCT and QFT is the performance cost of the QFT's \emph{larger, softer} block: its block side is twice that of JPEG's and its boundaries are not sharp.

\subsection{Where the block prior fails: per-image variance}
\label{sec:per_image}

While the representative image of \cref{tab:div2k_repr} reflects typical DIV2K test-set behavior, a small number of images push hard against the block-prior assumption.
The clearest counterexample in our evaluation set is an image dominated by long-range, whole-frame correlations (e.g.\ a sweeping landscape with no block-local texture).
At 20\% keep, the four transforms on this counterexample achieve FFT 24.03, DCT 25.31, BlockDCT 25.99, QFT 24.21 dB (PSNR).
The BlockDCT $-$ DCT gap shrinks to $+0.68$~dB, compared with the $\sim\!+4$~dB seen on the representative image and averaged across the rest of the test set: the block-structure prior gains almost nothing over a full-image DCT here.
This is also the only image in our evaluation set where the learned QFT underperforms full-image DCT ($-1.10$~dB).

The QFT's failure on this counterexample is therefore not a failure of training: it is the \emph{same} failure mode as BlockDCT.
Images whose dominant correlations span the whole frame (rather than being block-local) are not well-served by any transform whose coefficient selection is structured by blocks, and the trained QFT inherits this weakness precisely because its learned prior \emph{is} a block prior.
Future work that removes the block assumption---for example by adapting the frozen-vs-mixing pattern \emph{per image} or via multi-scale parameterizations---would specifically target this regime.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The striking feature of~\cref{tab:div2k_repr} is not that any individual learned circuit beats FFT or DCT---the basic-variant circuits essentially tie full-image DCT---but rather that the dominant performance variable is \emph{block size}, not basis.
Moving from a full-image Fourier basis to a full-image cosine basis gains $\sim\!1.5$~dB at 20\% keep; moving from a full-image cosine basis to an $8 \times 8$ block-wise cosine basis gains another $\sim\!3.2$~dB.
This is consistent with decades of block-transform coding experience in image and video compression~\cite{wallace1992jpeg}: block-locality is a strong image-statistics prior, and coefficient selection benefits enormously from being confined to local tiles.
Our contribution is to place parametric quantum circuits on the same axis: the basic-variant QFT manifold, when trained, leaves the full-image endpoint and discovers an intermediate point at effective block size $2^4 = 16$ pixels (\cref{sec:emergence}); explicitly wrapping the same circuit in an $8 \times 8$ block prior (\cref{sec:block_wrapper}) lifts the best learned PSNR to within $-0.3$~dB of BlockDCT.

The structural analysis in~\cref{sec:emergence} makes the block-emergence concrete: 7 of the 16 Hadamard-role gates in the trained QFT collapse to near-Pauli matrices, and the collapse is quantized rather than smooth.
The remaining Hadamards and all 56 controlled-phase gates carry rich parameterization, so the intra-block transform is fully exploited; the transform's block structure comes entirely from the frozen Hadamards.
A clean way to read this is that training has performed an implicit model selection on the block-size axis: starting from the full-image DFT (no frozen Hadamards) and optimizing under the MSE reconstruction objective, gradient descent has moved the solution to a point at which some fraction of the mixing qubits have become classical block indices.
That the stopping point is 4 frozen bits per dimension---corresponding to JPEG-$2 \times$---rather than 3 (JPEG-equivalent) or 5 (MPEG-equivalent), is a property of natural-image statistics as seen through the DIV2K training distribution.

Two factors account for the residual $\sim\!3$~dB gap that the unblocked QFT shows against BlockDCT.
First, its effective block is twice as large as JPEG's ($16 \times 16$ vs $8 \times 8$), so the coefficient budget per block is four times larger and intra-block compaction is correspondingly weaker.
Second, the unblocked QFT's block boundaries are \emph{soft}: the off-diagonal magnitudes of the frozen Hadamards sit at the $\sim\!1\%$ level rather than zero, so a small amount of cross-block mixing remains.
The block-wrapped variants of \cref{sec:block_wrapper} close both: the wrapper makes the boundary exact, and a hand-chosen $b = 8$ matches the JPEG grid; combined with the richer per-gate manifold of RichBasis, this brings the learned basis to within $-0.3$~dB of BlockDCT.

The topology ranking in~\cref{tab:div2k_repr}---the four learned circuits sitting within $\sim 1$~dB of one another---is best read as a ranking \emph{within} the full-image unitary family.
The Entangled QFT's $n$ cross-dimensional phases outperform TEBD's $2n$ ring-topology phases because they connect degrees of freedom that are relevant to the 2D structure of images; TEBD's local ring connectivity is simply the wrong inductive bias for natural images.
The MERA-inspired basis, with hierarchical multi-scale connectivity, lies between these two.
All of this is orthogonal to the block-size axis: the Entangled QFT can be composed with a block structure, and the resulting basis would inherit both advantages.
Building and training such an architecture is the most natural next step.

Although we evaluate circuits classically as tensor networks, the parametric QFT structure maps directly to quantum hardware: implementing the Entangled QFT requires only $n$ additional CPHASE gates beyond the standard QFT circuit~\cite{luo2020yao, markov2008simulating}.
The structural finding that trained Hadamards collapse to Pauli-like gates suggests a \emph{compilation} benefit: on real quantum hardware, the frozen qubits could be replaced by their discrete Pauli equivalents, removing parameters and gate noise from those sites entirely.
On the classical side, the framework is released as an open-source Julia library, \texttt{ParametricDFT.jl}, at \url{https://github.com/nzy1997/ParametricDFT.jl}.

The whole-frame counterexample (\cref{sec:per_image}) is an honest counterweight: on images whose dominant correlations span the whole frame, all block-structured priors---hand-designed or learned---underperform full-image transforms.
Our QFT inherits BlockDCT's weakness by construction, since its learned prior \emph{is} a block prior.
Ablation studies isolating the contribution of Riemannian vs.\ projected Euclidean optimization, comparison against wavelets and learned dictionaries, and an explicit per-image adaptive block-selection mechanism are all natural follow-up directions.

\section{Conclusion}

We have introduced a continuous family of parametric quantum circuit bases for image compression, obtained by relaxing the Cooley--Tukey FFT's Hadamard and controlled-phase gates into learnable elements of $U(2)$ and $U(1)^4$ and optimizing the result on the Riemannian manifold of unitaries.
The family places the DFT at one endpoint of a block-size axis and permits gradient-based training to move the basis along that axis.

On DIV2K at $256 \times 256$ resolution, the basic-variant trained QFT essentially ties full-image DCT at 20\% compression (30.91 vs.\ 30.85 dB) and falls ${\sim}3.1$~dB behind $8 \times 8$ BlockDCT; wrapping the same circuit in an explicit $8 \times 8$ block grid (Blocked RealRichBasis) lifts it to within $-0.3$~dB of BlockDCT.
Inspecting the trained gate parameters explains this intermediate position: 7 of the 16 Hadamard-role gates collapse to near-Pauli matrices during training, freezing 4 of the 8 qubits per dimension into classical block indices and factoring the transform into blocks of side $2^{4} = 16$ pixels.
The learned transform has spontaneously performed an implicit model selection on the block-size axis, landing at twice JPEG's block size rather than at the full image.

Within the full-image unitary family we confirm a stable topology ranking: Entangled QFT beats the separable QFT, which beats the MERA-inspired basis, which beats TEBD.
Combining the cross-dimensional coupling of the Entangled QFT with an explicit block prior, so that block size is a learnable structural parameter rather than an emergent byproduct, is the natural next step.

The framework unifies tensor network contraction, Riemannian optimization, and differentiable programming into a single pipeline.
New circuit topologies can be defined by specifying a connectivity pattern; the manifold optimization, batched einsum evaluation, and differentiable truncation machinery apply unchanged.

\paragraph{Beyond natural images.}
Although our experiments target DIV2K, the framework's only structural assumption is that signals admit a sparse representation in some unitary basis---a setting in which a fixed FFT or DCT is currently the standard tool.
Three application domains stand out as natural next targets.
\emph{Medical imaging} relies on JPEG/JPEG-2000 for archive compression of CT, MRI, and OCT slices, and on fixed Fourier sensing matrices for compressed-sensing MRI~\cite{lustig2007sparse}; replacing the fixed transform with one trained per anatomical site gives a basis whose effective block size matches the structural scale of the modality, and exposes the sensing matrix itself to gradient-based co-design with the reconstructor.
\emph{Hyperspectral satellite imagery} is dominated by spatial--spectral block-DCT codecs~\cite{rusyn2016hyperspectral} that ignore the strong spectral correlations a learned per-band unitary can capture.
\emph{Audio coding} is built around the modified DCT (MDCT) at the heart of MP3, AAC, and Opus~\cite{princen1986mdct}; the parametric circuit family extends naturally to 1D and gives the same continuous interpolation between full-signal Fourier and block-local detection windows that proved decisive on images.
In all three settings, the existing hand-designed transform serves as a strong baseline with a published evaluation protocol, so the methodology of this paper transfers directly.
The release of \texttt{ParametricDFT.jl} as an open-source library is intended to make these extensions straightforward.

\section{Riemannian optimization details}
\label{app:riemannian}

\subsection{Cayley retraction derivation}

For a unitary matrix $U \in U(2)$ and tangent vector $\xi = US$ where $S^\dagger = -S$, the Cayley retraction~\cite{wen2013feasible, li2020efficient, tagare2011notes} maps:
\begin{align}
  W &= \xi U^\dagger = USU^\dagger, \\
  W &\leftarrow \frac{W - W^\dagger}{2} \quad \text{(project to } \mathfrak{u}(2)\text{)}, \\
  R_U(\alpha \xi) &= \left(I - \frac{\alpha}{2}W\right)^{-1}\left(I + \frac{\alpha}{2}W\right)U.
\end{align}

Since $W$ is skew-Hermitian, $(I - \frac{\alpha}{2}W)^{-1}(I + \frac{\alpha}{2}W)$ is unitary for any $\alpha \in \RR$, as can be verified by checking that the product with its adjoint equals the identity.

For $2 \times 2$ matrices, the inverse admits a closed form via the adjugate:
\begin{equation}
  (I - \frac{\alpha}{2}W)^{-1} = \frac{1}{\det(I - \frac{\alpha}{2}W)} \operatorname{adj}(I - \frac{\alpha}{2}W).
\end{equation}

\subsection{Parallel transport for Riemannian Adam}

For $U(2)$, the projection-based transport from $U_t$ to $U_{t+1}$ is:
\begin{equation}
  \Gamma_{U_t \to U_{t+1}}(m_t) = U_{t+1} \cdot \skew(U_{t+1}^\dagger m_t).
\end{equation}

For $U(1)^4$, the transport is the tangent projection at the new point:
\begin{equation}
  \Gamma_{\mathbf{z}_t \to \mathbf{z}_{t+1}}(m_t) = i \cdot \operatorname{Im}(\bar{\mathbf{z}}_{t+1} \circ m_t) \circ \mathbf{z}_{t+1}.
\end{equation}

Both are exact to first order in the step size.

\section{Alternative circuit topologies: TEBD and MERA}
\label{app:alt_topologies}

This appendix gives the gate-level construction of the two connectivity foils summarized in~\cref{sec:alt_topologies}.

\subsection{TEBD basis}

Time-Evolving Block Decimation~\cite{vidal2004tebd} uses a \emph{ring topology} of nearest-neighbor controlled-phase gates $T_k = \diag(1, 1, 1, e^{i\phi_k})$ preceded by Hadamard gates on each qubit, with wrap-around closing each register's ring independently.
The row and column rings have $n$ phase gates each ($2n$ learnable phases total) and the circuit evaluates in $O(n)$ gate operations per dimension, the cheapest of the four topologies at inference.
The parameter manifold is $\MM_{\text{TEBD}} = \prod_{k=1}^{2n} U(1)^4$.
TEBD's purpose in our comparison is to isolate connectivity from parameter count: it has twice as many phase parameters as the Entangled QFT's entanglement gates but arranges them with strictly local connectivity (\cref{fig:alt_topologies}, left).

\subsection{MERA-inspired basis}

Multi-scale Entanglement Renormalization~\cite{vidal2007mera} uses hierarchical connectivity with disentanglers and isometries at increasing spatial scales.
Our MERA-inspired basis borrows the connectivity pattern but parameterizes each gate as $\diag(1, 1, 1, e^{i\phi})$ for consistency with the other bases and to preserve invertibility.
For $n = 2^k$ qubits, the circuit has $\log_2 n$ layers with stride $2^{l-1}$ in layer $l$, totalling $2(n-1)$ gates per dimension and $O(\log n)$ depth.
The hierarchical connectivity captures multi-scale features: layer~1 mixes nearest neighbors, deeper layers mix distant qubits.
MERA's purpose in our comparison is to test whether \emph{multi-scale} connectivity (a weaker form of all-to-all) suffices to match the Entangled QFT; \cref{sec:topology_div2k} shows that it does not (\cref{fig:alt_topologies}, right).
