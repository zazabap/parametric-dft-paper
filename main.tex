% ABSTRACT

\begin{abstract}
Is the Fourier basis (the Discrete Fourier Transform [DFT], the Discrete Cosine Transform [DCT], and their block-wise variants used by JPEG and the High Efficiency Video Coding [HEVC] standard) the optimal unitary transform for image compression, or merely the most mathematically tractable? To answer this question, we relax the gates of the Quantum Fourier Transform and its connectivity variants into free unitaries. The result is a parametric quantum-circuit family of image bases with $O(N \log N)$ forward- and backward-pass cost per image. Seven circuit structures (four full-image and three $8 \times 8$ block-wrapped) are benchmarked against fixed and dataset-fitted classical baselines on DIV2K natural images and Quick Draw line drawings. The answer depends on source statistics. On Quick Draw, the empirical lag-1 autocorrelation $\hat{\rho}_{\mathrm{AR}} \approx 0.73$ sits far from the first-order autoregressive (AR(1))--Gaussian limit at which the DCT is asymptotically optimal. There, the trained block-wrapped basis exceeds JPEG's $8 \times 8$ BlockDCT by $+6$~dB at $20\%$ retention. On DIV2K, where the source is approximately AR(1)--Gaussian, the same family attains parity with BlockDCT to within $0.31$~dB. The framework is released as the open-source Python library \texttt{pdft}, with a companion Julia implementation \texttt{ParametricDFT.jl}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Every major image codec, from JPEG~\cite{wallace1992jpeg} to the High Efficiency Video Coding (HEVC) standard~\cite{sullivan2012hevc}, projects images onto a fixed unitary basis, keeps the largest coefficients, and discards the rest~\cite{goyal2001transform, mallat2008wavelet}; the basis is the single most consequential design choice in the pipeline.
In practice that choice has remained narrow: the Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT)~\cite{cooley1965fft}, the Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT)~\cite{ahmed1974dct}, and their block-wise variants have dominated every deployed image and video codec for decades.

A parallel literature describes linear transforms on high-dimensional arrays in a different language.
Tensor networks were introduced as compact descriptions of quantum many-body states, including hierarchical constructions such as the Multi-scale Entanglement Renormalization Ansatz (MERA)~\cite{vidal2007mera} and time-evolution protocols such as Time-Evolving Block Decimation (TEBD)~\cite{vidal2004tebd}, and have since been adopted as machine-learning models for classification~\cite{stoudenmire2016supervised}, generative modeling~\cite{han2018unsupervised}, and array compression via tensor-train decomposition~\cite{oseledets2011tensor}, aided by differentiable contraction pipelines on GPU~\cite{liu2021tropical, liu2023generic, OMEinsum}.
Parameterized quantum circuits emerged as the workhorse of variational quantum algorithms~\cite{mcclean2016variational, cerezo2021variational} and quantum machine learning~\cite{schuld2019quantum, benedetti2019parameterized}, offering a disciplined way to search over unitary transforms despite barren-plateau challenges~\cite{mcclean2018barren, cerezo2021cost}.
The observation closing the circle is that the DFT itself is a quantum circuit, the Quantum Fourier Transform, whose Hadamard and controlled-phase gates exactly implement the Cooley--Tukey butterfly; relaxing those gates turns basis selection into a search over a compact parametric circuit.

Two obstacles have kept this program from being carried out.
End-to-end learned codecs~\cite{balle2017end, theis2017} sidestep the question by absorbing everything into a convolutional autoencoder; block-transform learning~\cite{pons2024rdlt} parameterizes in unconstrained Euclidean space and fixes the block size, trading away the unitarity that underwrites transform coding.
A direct gradient search over unitary bases compounds the issue: unitarity is a hard manifold constraint requiring Riemannian optimization on matrix manifolds~\cite{edelman1998geometry, absil2008optimization, boumal2023introduction, becigneul2019riemannian, kasai2019riemannian, khaneja2005optimal, wen2013feasible}, and the top-$k$ coefficient selection that defines transform coding is non-differentiable~\cite{bengio2013estimating}.

We resolve both obstacles by choosing parameterization and optimizer jointly.
Relaxing each Hadamard into a free element of $U(2)$ and each controlled-phase into $U(1)^4$ yields a circuit that is unitary by construction, has $O(\log^2 N)$ parameters in the image side $N$, and contains the DFT as a single point.
The product manifold is searched with Riemannian Adam~\cite{kingma2015adam, becigneul2019riemannian} using Cayley retractions~\cite{wen2013feasible, li2020efficient} that preserve unitarity at any step size; top-$k$ non-differentiability is handled by a straight-through estimator~\cite{bengio2013estimating}, and the whole pipeline runs as a batched einsum contraction under automatic differentiation~\cite{innes2018dont, OMEinsum, luo2020yao, liao2019differentiable}.
Three contributions follow.
(i)~\textbf{A parametric quantum circuit family of unitary image bases with $O(N \log N)$ cost.} Relaxing each gate of the Cooley--Tukey FFT yields a continuous family on the product manifold $\prod_g \bigl(U(2) \times U(1)^4\bigr)$ that contains the FFT, the full-image DCT, and BlockDCT as distinguished points and inherits FFT-cost forward and backward passes; Riemannian optimization moves the basis continuously between them.
(ii)~\textbf{Source-regime-dependent performance.} On DIV2K~\cite{div2k} natural images, well approximated by a stationary AR(1)--Gaussian source where the Ahmed--Jain DCT-to-KLT (Karhunen--Lo\`{e}ve Transform) equivalence is asymptotic~\cite{ahmed1974dct, jain1979sinusoidal}, the learned $8 \times 8$ block-wrapped basis matches JPEG's BlockDCT to within $0.31$~dB at $20\%$ retention, attaining parity with the asymptotically optimal classical reference. On Quick Draw~\cite{quickdraw} line drawings, where the AR(1) assumption fails, the same family outperforms every classical baseline tested (BlockDCT, BD-PCA, BlockFFT) by at least $+5.0$~dB at the same retention; per-image training takes approximately five minutes for a $500$-image training slice on a single RTX~3090. Gate-level inspection of the trained basic-variant QFT reveals the underlying structure: half of the Hadamard-role gates per dimension collapse to within $\sim\!1\%$ of Pauli matrices at convergence, factoring the trained transform into an effective $16 \times 16$-pixel block code without an explicit block prior.
(iii)~\textbf{Open-source release.} The framework is implemented as the Python library \texttt{pdft} (the primary development target) and the companion Julia library \texttt{ParametricDFT.jl}, both of which combine tensor-network contraction, Riemannian optimization, and differentiable programming for unitary basis search.

The rest of the paper proceeds as follows.
\Cref{sec:circuits} defines the four circuit topologies, \cref{sec:training} the Riemannian search, and \cref{sec:experiments} reports the main results and the gate-level analysis (\cref{sec:emergence}).
\Cref{sec:discussion} discusses implications.
The derivation of the parametric circuit from the Cooley--Tukey FFT is collected in \cref{sec:background}.

\section{Parametric Circuit Topologies}
\label{sec:circuits}

Each topology defines a parametric unitary transform $\TT(\boldsymbol{\theta}): \CC^{2^m \times 2^n} \to \CC^{2^m \times 2^n}$ over a common gate parameterization: Hadamard-like gates $H \in U(2)$ and controlled-phase gates $M = \diag(1, 1, 1, e^{i\phi}) \in U(1)^4$.
Setting all parameters to their QFT values recovers the classical DFT.
We organize the seven learned bases reported in this paper into two subsections: four \emph{basic variants} (\cref{sec:basic_variants}) act on the full image as a single unitary, while three \emph{block-wrapped variants} (\cref{sec:block_wrapper}) tile a richer inner circuit across $b \times b$ tiles of the image.

\subsection{Basic variants}
\label{sec:basic_variants}

The basic variants act on the full image as a single $2^m \times 2^n$ unitary; they differ only in how the controlled-phase chain is wired (\cref{tab:circuits}).
The \emph{separable QFT} (\cref{sec:qft_basis}) directly parameterizes the tensor network of \cref{sec:gate_decomposition}, with $m + n$ Hadamard gates on $U(2)$ and $m(m-1)/2 + n(n-1)/2$ controlled-phase gates on $U(1)^4$, giving the product transform $\TT = F_m(\boldsymbol{\theta}_{\text{row}}) \otimes F_n(\boldsymbol{\theta}_{\text{col}})$.
Setting all parameters to their QFT values recovers the classical DFT.

\paragraph{Entangled QFT.}
\label{sec:qft_basis}
The separable QFT processes row and column dimensions independently, ignoring spatial correlations between them.
Natural images carry strong cross-dimensional correlations (diagonal edges, radial textures), so we add $n$ entanglement gates $E_k = \diag(1, 1, 1, e^{i\phi_k})$ coupling the qubit pairs $(x_{n-k}, y_{n-k})$ (indexed from the least-significant pair at $k=1$ to the most-significant at $k=n$):
\begin{align}
  \TT_{\text{entangled}} &= U_{\text{entangle}} \cdot (F_n \otimes F_n), \nonumber \\
  U_{\text{entangle}} &= \prod_{k=1}^n E_k.
  \label{eq:entangled_qft}
\end{align}
The construction adds only $n$ parameters, keeps the per-dimension cost at $O(N \log N)$, and reduces to the separable QFT at $\phi_k = 0$ (\cref{fig:entangled_qft}).

Entanglement gates $E_k$ couple corresponding row--column pairs, introducing cross-dimensional correlations.}

\paragraph{TEBD and MERA controls.}
\label{sec:alt_topologies}
To stress-test the claim that cross-dimensional, all-to-all coupling is the decisive structural ingredient, we add two connectivity foils: a TEBD-style~\cite{vidal2004tebd} \emph{ring} (nearest-neighbor controlled-phase gates with wrap-around, $O(n)$ depth, $2n$ phase parameters total) and a MERA-inspired~\cite{vidal2007mera} \emph{hierarchical} circuit (disentanglers and isometries at increasing strides, $O(\log n)$ depth, $4(n-1)$ phase parameters).
Both share the same $U(2)$ Hadamard layer as the QFT but replace its all-to-all phase chain with local or multi-scale connectivity.
Their gate-level construction and circuit diagrams are deferred to \cref{app:alt_topologies}; we use them purely as controls in the topology comparison of \cref{sec:topology_div2k}.

\subsection{Block-wrapped variants}
\label{sec:block_wrapper}

The four topologies above act on the full image as a single $2^m \times 2^n$ unitary.
A second design axis, motivated directly by the $8 \times 8$ block-based DCT used in JPEG~\cite{wallace1992jpeg}, wraps any of these inner circuits in an explicit block partition: the image is reshaped into a grid of $b \times b$ tiles and the same parametric circuit $U(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ is applied independently to each tile,
\begin{equation}
  \TT_{\text{block}}(\boldsymbol{\theta})(\mathbf{x})_{[i,j]}
  = U(\boldsymbol{\theta})\, \mathbf{x}_{[i,j]},
  \quad \mathbf{x}_{[i,j]} \in \CC^{b \times b},
  \label{eq:block_wrapper}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{x}_{[i,j]}$ is the $(i,j)$-th block.
The wrapper is generic over the inner topology. We report three block-wrapped bases at $b = 8$, organized as a hierarchy of within-block parameter richness (\cref{fig:block_basis_circuits}): the Cooley--Tukey-derived \emph{Blocked QFT} ($15$ real params/dim) recovers the QFT structure inside each tile; \emph{RichBasis} ($H + U(4)$, $57$ params/dim) replaces each two-qubit gate with a free $U(4)$; and the headline \emph{RealRichBasis} ($H + O(4)$, $21$ params/dim) constrains those gates to real-orthogonal $O(4)$, matching RichBasis at half the parameter count.

\begin{figure}[!tbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{block_basis_circuits.pdf}
\caption{Block-wrapped parametric basis (\cref{sec:block_wrapper}) viewed as a hierarchy on the inner $b \times b$ within-block transform ($m = 3$ qubits/dim shown). Top: RealRichBasis ($H + O^{(4)}$, $21$ real params/dim) is the headline learned basis. \label{fig:block_basis_circuits}
\end{figure}

The block size $b$ is the structural prior: $b = N$ recovers the full-image circuits already defined; $b = 8$ matches the JPEG block grid.
The unblocked separable QFT, after training on natural images, exhibits a block-structured factorization: several Hadamard-role gates have collapsed to near-Pauli matrices at convergence, leaving the corresponding qubits acting as classical block indices, as detailed in \cref{sec:emergence}.
The block structure observed in the trained basic-variant motivates offering an explicit block prior as a complementary, hand-designed structural choice; the experiments in \cref{sec:topology_div2k,sec:blockprior_results} report results for both.

\section{Training Objective and Optimization}
\label{sec:training}

\subsection{Loss function}
\label{sec:loss}

All reported results are trained under the Mean Squared Error (MSE) reconstruction loss
\begin{equation}
  \LL_{\text{MSE}}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \bigl\| \mathbf{x} - \TT(\boldsymbol{\theta})^{\dagger}\, \topk\!\bigl(\TT(\boldsymbol{\theta})\,\mathbf{x},\, k\bigr) \bigr\|_F^2,
  \label{eq:mse_loss}
\end{equation}
where $\TT(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ is the parametric unitary basis (so $\TT^\dagger = \TT^{-1}$) and $\topk(\cdot, k)$ retains the $k$ coefficients with the highest frequency-weighted score (\cref{sec:truncation}), zeroing the rest. The symbol $k$ here denotes the retention count rather than the qubit count of \cref{sec:gate_decomposition}.
The objective is end-to-end: gradients flow from reconstruction error through the truncation layer (\cref{sec:topk_grad}) and back to the transform parameters.
Two natural alternatives, the $\ell_1$ and $\ell_2$ norms applied directly to the transform output, are reported in \cref{app:alt_losses}.

\subsection{Frequency-weighted truncation}
\label{sec:truncation}

The top-$k$ selection ranks coefficients by a frequency-weighted score rather than raw magnitude:
\begin{equation}
  s_{i,j} = |y_{i,j}| \cdot (1 + w_{i,j}),
  \quad
  w_{i,j} = 1 - \frac{d_{i,j}}{2\, d_{\max}},
\end{equation}
where $d_{i,j} = \sqrt{(i - c_i)^2 + (j - c_j)^2}$ is the Euclidean distance from frequency bin $(i,j)$ to the DC bin $(c_i, c_j)$, and $d_{\max}$ is its maximum over the spectrum.
Low-frequency coefficients receive up to a $2{\times}$ boost in retention score, consistent with empirical compressibility under perceptual quality metrics.

\subsection{Straight-through gradient for top-$k$}
\label{sec:topk_grad}

Top-$k$ selection is non-differentiable: the set $\mathcal{S} \subseteq \{1, \dots, N^2\}$ of retained indices changes discontinuously with $\mathbf{y}$.
We adopt the \emph{straight-through estimator}~\cite{bengio2013estimating}, which passes gradients through retained coefficients and zeros them elsewhere:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial\, \topk(\mathbf{y}, k)_{i,j}}{\partial y_{i,j}} =
  \begin{cases}
    1 & (i,j) \in \mathcal{S}, \\
    0 & \text{otherwise}.
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:ste}
\end{equation}
The estimator is justified by three observations.
First, the exact Jacobian carries delta-function contributions at boundaries between mask configurations, which are unusable for gradient-based optimization.
Second, $\mathcal{S}$ is locally stable along the optimization trajectory, so~\cref{eq:ste} agrees with the exact derivative away from those boundaries.
Third, the resulting gradient correctly encodes the instruction \emph{adjust the retained coefficients to better approximate the signal}.
Continuous relaxations of subset selection, such as the Gumbel-softmax reparameterization~\cite{jang2017categorical} and reparameterizable subset sampling~\cite{xie2019reparameterizable}, are alternative options we did not find necessary in this setting.

\subsection{Riemannian Adam}
\label{sec:riemannian}

The circuit parameters live on two matrix manifolds: the unitary group $U(2)$ for Hadamard-role gates and the phase torus $U(1)^4$ for controlled-phase gates.
Their tangent spaces at $U \in U(2)$ and $\mathbf{z} \in U(1)^4$ are
\begin{align}
  T_U\, U(2) &= \{ U S : S^\dagger = -S \}, \\
  T_{\mathbf{z}}\, U(1)^4 &= \{ i \boldsymbol{\theta} \circ \mathbf{z} : \boldsymbol{\theta} \in \RR^4 \},
\end{align}
following the standard matrix-manifold conventions~\cite{edelman1998geometry, absil2008optimization, boumal2023introduction, zhang2016first}.
Given the Euclidean gradient $\nabla_E f$ supplied by automatic differentiation, the Riemannian gradient is the projection onto the tangent space:
\begin{align}
  \grad f(U) &= U \cdot \skew(U^\dagger \nabla_E f), \label{eq:grad_u2} \\
  \grad f(\mathbf{z}) &= i \cdot \operatorname{Im}(\bar{\mathbf{z}} \circ \nabla_E f) \circ \mathbf{z}, \label{eq:grad_u1}
\end{align}
where $\skew(A) = (A - A^\dagger)/2$ extracts the skew-Hermitian part.
Updates are pulled back to the manifold by a retraction $R$: the Cayley map for $U(2)$, which preserves unitarity exactly at any step size (\cref{app:cayley}), and elementwise normalization for $U(1)^4$.

All reported results use Riemannian Adam~\cite{kingma2015adam, becigneul2019riemannian, kasai2019riemannian} as the optimizer.
First- and second-moment estimates of the Riemannian gradient are maintained with $\beta_1 = 0.9$ and $\beta_2 = 0.999$, and momentum is parallel-transported between successive tangent spaces by projection (\cref{app:parallel_transport}).
The bias-corrected update direction is $d_t = \hat{m}_t / (\sqrt{\hat{v}_t} + \epsilon)$ with $\epsilon = 10^{-8}$.
Step size and learning-rate schedule are listed in~\cref{app:benchmark}.

Parameter-shift rules for hardware-quantum gradients~\cite{mitarai2018quantum, schuld2019evaluating} and the quantum natural gradient~\cite{stokes2020quantum} are not required here: classical simulation supplies Euclidean gradients via reverse-mode automatic differentiation at negligible cost.
A vanilla Riemannian gradient-descent variant with Armijo line search, used during early development, is documented in \cref{app:armijo_gd}.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

We evaluate on two datasets:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Quick Draw}~\cite{quickdraw}: Hand-drawn sketches ($28 \times 28$, zero-padded to $32 \times 32$, \ie $m = n = 5$ qubits). A small-scale sanity check.
  \item \textbf{DIV2K}~\cite{div2k}: High-resolution natural photographs, preprocessed to $256 \times 256$ ($m = n = 8$ qubits). This is the main benchmark; reported results use a generalized training preset over a 500-image training slice.
\end{itemize}

The two datasets sit at distinct positions on the AR(1) source-statistics axis (\cref{fig:ar1_histogram}): DIV2K clusters near the AR(1)--Gaussian limit at which the DCT is asymptotically optimal~\cite{ahmed1974dct, jain1979sinusoidal}, while Quick Draw sits well below it.

\begin{figure}[!tbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{ar1_histogram.pdf}
\caption{Distribution of the empirical lag-1 autocorrelation $\hat{\rho}_{\mathrm{AR}} = \tfrac{1}{2}(\hat{\rho}_{\mathrm{row}} + \hat{\rho}_{\mathrm{col}})$ across the 500-image training slice of each dataset. DIV2K natural photographs concentrate near the stationary Gaussian AR(1)--KLT limit ($\hat{\rho}_{\mathrm{AR}} \to 1$, mean $\hat{\rho}_{\mathrm{AR}} = 0.92$), where BlockDCT is asymptotically the optimal linear basis; Quick Draw drawings sit clearly below that limit (mean $\hat{\rho}_{\mathrm{AR}} = 0.73$), leaving room for a learned basis to outperform BlockDCT.}
\label{fig:ar1_histogram}
\end{figure}

All learned bases are trained per image using Riemannian Adam (\cref{sec:riemannian}) on the MSE loss (\cref{eq:mse_loss}) with $k = 10\%$ coefficients retained during training. The remaining hyperparameters (cosine LR schedule, batch size, validation split, seed) are listed in~\cref{app:benchmark}.

We report Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio (PSNR; $10 \log_{10}(1/\text{MSE})$) at retention ratios $\rho \in \{0.05, 0.10, 0.15, 0.20\}$, averaged over the 50-image test slice of each dataset.
Every method evaluated here uses \emph{top-$k$} truncation: the $k = \lfloor \rho N \rfloor$ coefficients with the largest absolute magnitude per image are retained and the rest zeroed, matching how JPEG-style codecs entropy-code only the surviving coefficients.
The alternative \emph{rank} rule (keep the first $k$ coefficients in a fixed ordering, \eg the highest-eigenvalue directions of Principal Component Analysis (PCA)) under which a KLT-derived basis attains the lowest MSE by construction matches top-$k$ only in the AR(1)--Gaussian asymptote~\cite{effros2004suboptimality}.
PCA is therefore variance-optimal under rank truncation, while the trained PDFT bases are sparsity-optimal under top-$k$ truncation, and on natural-image distributions the latter governs reconstruction quality at a fixed coefficient budget.

We compare against the following classical references along the \emph{block-size axis}:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{FFT}, \textbf{DCT}: the full-image DFT and DCT-II; closed-form, no data fit. FFT is the un-learned initialization of every QFT-family basis.
  \item \textbf{BlockFFT}, \textbf{BlockDCT} ($8 \times 8$): the same transforms applied independently to each $8 \times 8$ tile; BlockDCT is the JPEG~\cite{wallace1992jpeg} reference.
  \item \textbf{BD-PCA}, \textbf{BlockBD-PCA} ($8 \times 8$): bilateral 2D-PCA with row and column eigenbases fitted on the training slice and applied as $\Phi(\mathbf{x}) = U^\top \mathbf{x} V$, full-image and $8 \times 8$-tiled respectively.
\end{itemize}

End-to-end learned codecs~\cite{balle2017end, theis2017} are not included as baselines: they couple a non-unitary nonlinear encoder with entropy coding, so their rate is measured in bits-per-pixel rather than the retained-coefficient ratio $\rho$ used here.
A like-for-like comparison would require a fixed-protocol common axis (e.g., bits-per-pixel at matched reconstruction MSE) and is left to follow-up work.

\subsection{Topology comparison within the full-image unitary family}
\label{sec:topology_div2k}

We compare the seven learned bases against classical references on both datasets.
\Cref{tab:quickdraw_repr,tab:div2k_repr} report the headline result.
The headline learned-blocked basis is RealRichBasis on DIV2K, where it leads the learned-blocked subgroup, and RichBasis on Quick Draw, where it edges past RealRichBasis.

We first read off the DIV2K topology comparison.
The four learned basic-variant topologies (QFT, Entangled QFT, TEBD, MERA) sit within ${\sim}0.4$~dB of one another at every keep ratio, with TEBD and MERA tied at the top (\cref{tab:div2k_repr}, unblocked-learned subgroup).
The three additional learned bases produce 2D spectra and kept-coefficient masks that are visually indistinguishable from the separable QFT, so the figures in the remainder of this section show only the separable QFT as a representative member of the family.

\subsection{The block-size axis: QFT, DCT and BlockDCT}
\label{sec:blocksize}

\cref{tab:div2k_repr} is the central result of this paper.
Reading the fixed-baseline rows top-to-bottom traces the block-size axis from the full image down to an $8 \times 8$ JPEG block, with the learned QFT interpolating between full-image DCT and $8 \times 8$ BlockDCT.
Four effects are visible at 20\% keep (the column that matters most for practical compression):
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \emph{Data-adaptive orientation gives $+1.5$~dB.} Full-image DCT (30.85~dB) beats full-image FFT (29.39~dB) by $+1.5$~dB because its real-valued cosine basis aligns better with natural-image statistics than the DFT's complex basis, while the block size is the same.
  \item \emph{Block structure gives $+3.2$~dB.} BlockDCT at $8 \times 8$ (34.01~dB) beats full-image DCT by $+3.2$~dB; this is by far the largest single effect in the table, and it is driven entirely by the change in block size, not by a change in the basis within a block.
  \item \emph{Basic-variant training matches DCT but does not beat it.} The best basic-variant learned circuit (TEBD/MERA at 31.28~dB) sits within $+0.4$~dB of full-image DCT and $-2.7$~dB below BlockDCT; without an explicit block prior, the trained basic-variant circuits exhibit an effective block structure (\cref{sec:emergence}) but cannot close the BlockDCT gap on this dataset.
  \item \emph{Block-wrapping closes most of the BlockDCT gap.} Wrapping the same circuits in an $8 \times 8$ block (\cref{sec:block_wrapper}) lifts the best learned PSNR to 33.70~dB (Blocked RealRichBasis), $-0.3$~dB below BlockDCT and $+2.85$~dB above full-image DCT, so the learned blocked basis essentially ties the JPEG-equivalent fixed transform.
\end{enumerate}

The same axis decomposition on Quick Draw, where the AR(1) assumption fails, reorders the effects sharply.
At 20\% keep the data-adaptive orientation effect is preserved (DCT 22.03~dB vs.\ FFT 20.56~dB, $+1.5$~dB), but the remaining three diverge from the DIV2K story.
Block structure gives $+4.6$~dB (BlockDCT 26.63~dB vs.\ DCT), larger than DIV2K's $+3.2$~dB.
Basic-variant training gives $+2.3$~dB (QFT 24.35~dB vs.\ DCT), roughly six times the $+0.4$~dB on DIV2K, because once the source is no longer near-AR(1) the freedom to learn the basis is no longer redundant with the closed-form DCT.
Block-wrapping with richer within-block gates lifts the result by a further $+5.97$~dB (Blocked RichBasis 32.60~dB vs.\ BlockDCT 26.63~dB), which is the headline gap of the paper.
On non-AR(1) sources, the dominant lever is the within-block basis, not the block size: BlockDCT's hand-designed $8 \times 8$ cosine basis leaves roughly $6$~dB of sparsity on the table.

\paragraph{Why $b = 8$, defended on this paper's data.}
To defend the headline $b = 8$ we ran a full sweep across $b \in \{2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64, 128\}$ for the classical baselines and over the HEVC transform-unit set $\{4, 8, 16, 32\}$ (DIV2K) / $\{4, 8, 16\}$ (Quick Draw) for the trained block-wrapped bases, at four retention ratios $\rho \in \{0.05, 0.10, 0.15, 0.20\}$, training each cell at the same 1{,}008-step preset (\cref{app:block_size_sweep}).
At $\rho = 0.20$ on DIV2K, every dataset-fitted family (BlockDCT, BlockBD-PCA, RichBasis, RealRichBasis) peaks at $b = 8$, recovering the headline numbers in \cref{tab:div2k_repr} to $< 0.01$~dB.
The peak migrates with $\rho$ in two clear directions: at lower $\rho$ on DIV2K (more aggressive compression), $b = 16$ wins for the classical bases and $b = 32$ for the trained bases, because a tiny coefficient budget is best spent on a few low-frequency modes of a wider window; on Quick Draw, BlockDCT \emph{decreases monotonically} with $b$ at $\rho \ge 0.15$ (the all-white-background regime makes small tiles trivially compressible), while the dataset-fitted bases (BlockBD-PCA, RealRichBasis) peak at $b = 4$.
The optimal block size is therefore a 2D function of (content statistics, operating $\rho$); $b = 8$ is the empirical optimum at the paper's headline retention on natural-image content, the regime the headline tables actually evaluate.


\Cref{fig:freqrecon_compact_qd,fig:freqrecon_compact_div2k} translate the table numbers into visible quality on one representative image per dataset.
On Quick Draw, the trained block-wrapped basis (RealRichBasis) preserves recognizable stroke topology even at $\rho = 0.05$, where the full-image classical bases collapse to a blurred blob; on DIV2K, both block-wrapped methods (BlockDCT and the trained RealRichBasis) preserve fine architectural detail visibly better than full-image FFT or DCT at every $\rho$, and within the block-wrapped pair the trained basis is indistinguishable from the hand-designed BlockDCT to the eye.
The 13-method gallery in \cref{app:reconstruction_galleries} confirms the same story across the remaining bases.
The frequency-domain magnitudes in \cref{fig:freqrecon_compact_qd,fig:freqrecon_compact_div2k} make the block-size axis visual: full-image DCT energy compacts into the top-left corner of a single tile, BlockDCT tiles that same compaction into sharp $8 \times 8$ blocks, and the trained QFT produces a visibly coarser, softer pattern consistent with a $16 \times 16$-pixel effective block.
The position the trained QFT occupies between DCT and BlockDCT is not accidental, as \cref{sec:emergence} shows.

\subsection{Emergent block structure in the trained QFT}
\label{sec:emergence}

The learned QFT on $256 \times 256$ DIV2K discovers an effective $16 \times 16$ block structure: of the 16 Hadamard-role gates (8 per dimension), eight collapse during training to gates whose modulus pattern matches Pauli-$Z$ or Pauli-$X$ rather than the proper Hadamard $H = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\!\begin{pmatrix}1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1\end{pmatrix}$.
Whereas $H$ creates a DFT-butterfly superposition across the qubit's $|0\rangle / |1\rangle$ states, $Z$-like and $X$-like gates preserve computational-basis amplitudes (up to sign or bit flips) and no longer mix frequencies along that axis.
A frozen qubit therefore behaves as a \emph{classical block index}, and the transform factors into blocks of size $2^{m_{\mathrm{free}}} \times 2^{n_{\mathrm{free}}}$ pixels, where $m_{\mathrm{free}}$ and $n_{\mathrm{free}}$ are the still-mixing qubit counts per dimension.

In each of the row and column dimensions, four of the eight Hadamard-role gates retain their Hadamard role and the remaining four freeze, predominantly to $Z$-like (with one $X$-like in the column dimension).
The freezing is sharp rather than gradual: off-diagonal magnitudes of frozen gates sit at the ${\sim}1\%$ level, and all 56 controlled-phase gates retain their full $U(1)^4$ parameterization, so the intra-block transform remains rich.

\subsection{Where the block prior fails: per-image variance}
\label{sec:per_image}

While the representative image of \cref{tab:div2k_repr} reflects typical DIV2K test-set behavior, a small number of images push hard against the block-prior assumption.
The clearest counterexample in our evaluation set is an image dominated by long-range, whole-frame correlations (\eg a sweeping landscape with no block-local texture).
At 20\% keep, the four transforms on this counterexample achieve FFT 24.03, DCT 25.31, BlockDCT 25.99, QFT 24.21 dB (PSNR): the BlockDCT $-$ DCT gap shrinks from the $\sim\!+4$~dB test-set average to $+0.68$~dB, and this is the only image in our evaluation set where the learned QFT underperforms full-image DCT ($-1.10$~dB).
The QFT's failure here is the same failure mode as BlockDCT, not a failure of training (\cref{sec:discussion}).

\subsection{Adding an explicit block prior}
\label{sec:blockprior_results}

The block factorization observed in \cref{sec:emergence} suggests a complementary experiment: rather than relying on the trained basic-variant to land at an effective block size, we wrap the circuit in an explicit $b \times b$ partition (\cref{eq:block_wrapper}) and train the inner topology directly.
\Cref{tab:div2k_repr} contrasts the three unblocked topologies (QFT, Entangled QFT, TEBD) against the three block-wrapped variants (Blocked QFT, RichBasis, RealRichBasis) at $b = 8$, with BlockDCT~$8 \times 8$ as the closed-form reference.

Three observations follow.
First, the simplest block-wrapping (Blocked QFT, the QFT inner circuit applied per $8 \times 8$ tile) lifts the unblocked QFT by $+1.35$~dB on DIV2K and $+5.71$~dB on Quick Draw at $20\%$ keep, narrowing the gap to BlockDCT~$8 \times 8$ without changing the inner gates.
Second, replacing the diagonal controlled-phase gates with richer two-qubit gates closes the residual gap: RichBasis ($H + U(4)$) and RealRichBasis ($H + O(4)$) land within $0.31$~dB of BlockDCT~$8 \times 8$ on DIV2K (the parity expected on an AR(1)-like source) and exceed every classical baseline tested on Quick Draw by $\ge +5.0$~dB, including the strongest baseline BD-PCA.
Third, RealRichBasis matches RichBasis to within $0.05$~dB at less than half the parameter count: the imaginary parameters are not load-bearing on DIV2K.
The block prior that ties JPEG on AR(1)-like DIV2K is therefore the principal vehicle for the $+6$~dB gap on non-AR(1) Quick Draw, not a separate mechanism.
Training converges well within the 1{,}008-step budget on both datasets, and the trained block-wrapped bases reach a validation floor on Quick Draw roughly twice as low as on DIV2K, manifesting the headline $+6$~dB rate-distortion gap as training behavior; see \cref{app:training_dynamics} for per-epoch loss curves.

% DISCUSSION

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

The dominant performance variable in \cref{tab:div2k_repr} is block size, not basis (\cref{sec:blocksize}). The trained basic-variant QFT lands at the intermediate effective block size of $16$ pixels (\cref{sec:emergence}); wrapping the same circuit in an $8 \times 8$ block prior closes the gap to BlockDCT to within $-0.3$~dB, consistent with decades of block-transform coding experience~\cite{wallace1992jpeg, britanak2007}.

Two factors account for the residual $\sim\!3$~dB gap between the unblocked QFT and BlockDCT: the trained QFT's effective block is twice JPEG's (so its per-block budget is four times larger and intra-block compaction is correspondingly weaker), and its block boundaries are soft (\cref{sec:emergence}). The block-wrapped variants close both (exact boundaries plus the richer per-gate manifold of RichBasis), landing within $-0.3$~dB of BlockDCT.

The four learned topologies of \cref{tab:circuits} sit within $\sim\!1$~dB of one another, with $\leq 0.05$~dB between Entangled QFT, TEBD, and MERA: topology choice among reasonable variants contributes weakly compared to the block-size axis. Combining cross-dimensional coupling with an explicit block prior, so that $b$ becomes a learnable structural parameter rather than an emergent byproduct, is the natural follow-up.

Although we evaluate circuits classically, the parametric QFT structure could in principle be implemented on quantum hardware: the Entangled QFT adds only $n$ controlled-phase gates beyond the standard QFT~\cite{luo2020yao, markov2008simulating}, and the collapse of trained Hadamards to Pauli-like gates suggests a compilation benefit, since frozen qubits could be replaced by their discrete Pauli equivalents, removing parameters and gate noise from those sites.

A whole-frame counterexample (\cref{sec:per_image}) bounds the result: on images whose dominant correlations span the whole frame, all block-structured priors underperform full-image transforms, and the QFT inherits this weakness by construction. Comparison against wavelets and learned dictionaries, and an explicit per-image adaptive block-selection mechanism, are natural follow-ups.

% ============================================================================
% CONCLUSION
% ============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We have introduced a continuous parametric family of unitary image bases, obtained by relaxing the Hadamard and controlled-phase gates of the Cooley--Tukey FFT into free elements of $U(2)$ and $U(1)^4$ and optimizing on the Riemannian manifold of unitaries. The family contains the FFT, full-image DCT, and BlockDCT as distinguished points, and gradient-based training moves the basis continuously between them.

The performance is predicted by source regime. On DIV2K natural images, well approximated by an AR(1)--Gaussian source where BlockDCT is asymptotically optimal, the learned $8 \times 8$ block-wrapped basis matches JPEG's BlockDCT to within $0.31$~dB at $20\%$ retention. On Quick Draw line drawings, where the AR(1) assumption fails, the same family outperforms every classical baseline tested by at least $+5.0$~dB at the same retention, with per-image training under five minutes on a single RTX~3090.
Two limitations bound the result: per-image training is not yet a deployable codec, and content with whole-frame correlations (\cref{sec:per_image}) underperforms all block-structured priors, the trained QFT included.

\section{From Cooley--Tukey FFT to a Parametric Circuit}
\label{sec:background}

\begin{figure*}[!tbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{cooley_tukey_to_qft.pdf}
  \caption{Four transformations that turn the Cooley--Tukey FFT into a parametric circuit, drawn for $k = 4$ qubits ($N = 16$). (a)~\emph{Re-index}: the length-$N$ vector is read as a rank-$k$ tensor with one binary leg per qubit. (b)~\emph{Recurse}: one Cooley--Tukey level factors $F_n$ into a half-size DFT, a block-diagonal conditional twiddle, and an outer Hadamard on $q_0$. (c)~\emph{Decompose}: the conditional twiddle becomes a chain of $k{-}1$ controlled-phase gates $M_j$ coupling $q_0$ to each lower-significance qubit. (d)~\emph{Relax}: unrolling the recursion yields a circuit of $k$ Hadamards and $k(k-1)/2$ controlled-phase gates; relaxing each gate into a free element of $U(2)$ or $U(1)^4$ produces the parametric circuit trained in \cref{sec:circuits,sec:training}.}
  \label{fig:cooley_tukey_to_qft}
\end{figure*}

This appendix derives the parametric circuit that the rest of the paper trains.
Starting from the Cooley--Tukey FFT, we apply four local transformations to the underlying tensor network, namely \emph{re-index}, \emph{recurse}, \emph{decompose}, and \emph{relax}, yielding a circuit whose gates are free elements of the unitary manifold.
The construction is implemented in the Python library \texttt{pdft} and its Julia companion \texttt{ParametricDFT.jl}, both of which provide an executable derivation; \cref{fig:cooley_tukey_to_qft} shows the four transformations side by side for a $k = 4$-qubit ($N = 16$) example.

\subsection{Cooley--Tukey decomposition}

The DFT matrix of size $n = 2^k$ has entries $(F_n)_{i,j} = \omega^{(i-1)(j-1)}$ with $\omega = e^{-2\pi i / n}$ the primitive $n$-th root of unity, so that $F_n$ is the Vandermonde matrix
\begin{equation}
  F_n = \begin{pmatrix}
    1 & 1 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \\
    1 & \omega & \omega^2 & \cdots & \omega^{n-1} \\
    1 & \omega^2 & \omega^4 & \cdots & \omega^{2(n-1)} \\
    \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
    1 & \omega^{n-1} & \omega^{2(n-1)} & \cdots & \omega^{(n-1)^2}
  \end{pmatrix}.
  \label{eq:vandermonde}
\end{equation}
Split the columns by parity (odd $j = 1, 3, \ldots$; even $j = 2, 4, \ldots$) and the rows at $n/2$. Each of the four resulting $(n/2) \times (n/2)$ blocks reduces to the half-size DFT up to the twiddle $D_{n/2} = \diag(1, \omega, \ldots, \omega^{n/2-1})$:
\begin{equation}
  \begin{aligned}
    {[F_n]}^{\text{odd}}_{\text{top}} &= {[F_n]}^{\text{odd}}_{\text{bot}} = F_{n/2}, \\
    {[F_n]}^{\text{even}}_{\text{top}} &= -{[F_n]}^{\text{even}}_{\text{bot}} = D_{n/2}\, F_{n/2},
  \end{aligned}
  \label{eq:blocks}
\end{equation}
using $(\omega^2)^{(i-1)(j-1)} = (F_{n/2})_{i,j}$ for the odd columns, $\omega^n = 1$ for the bottom-odd block, and $\omega^{n/2} = -1$ for the bottom-even block.
Permuting $\mathbf{x}$ so that odd-indexed entries precede even-indexed entries and assembling the four blocks gives the Cooley--Tukey factorization
\begin{equation}
  F_n \mathbf{x}
  = \begin{pmatrix} I_{n/2} & D_{n/2} \\ I_{n/2} & -D_{n/2} \end{pmatrix}
    \begin{pmatrix} F_{n/2} & 0 \\ 0 & F_{n/2} \end{pmatrix}
    \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{x}_{\text{odd}} \\ \mathbf{x}_{\text{even}} \end{pmatrix}.
  \label{eq:fft}
\end{equation}
Applied recursively, each level reduces an $n$-point DFT to two $(n/2)$-point DFTs plus $O(n)$ twiddle multiplications, giving total cost $O(N \log N)$.
\Cref{eq:fft} is the input to the tensor-network construction.

\subsection{Building the parametric circuit}
\label{sec:gate_decomposition}

\emph{Re-index} (\cref{fig:cooley_tukey_to_qft}a). A vector of length $n = 2^k$ carries a single integer index that we write in binary as $i = q_0 + 2 q_1 + \cdots + 2^{k-1} q_{k-1}$.
Re-interpreting $\mathbf{x}$ as a rank-$k$ tensor with one binary leg $q_j$ per qubit is purely notational, but it turns every linear operator in~\cref{eq:fft} into a tensor-network component acting on specific qubit legs, which is what makes the gate decomposition below possible.

\emph{Recurse} (\cref{fig:cooley_tukey_to_qft}b). Take $q_0$ as the most-significant bit labelling the top and bottom row-blocks of~\cref{eq:fft}. The outer block matrix factors through the butterfly identity
\begin{equation}
  \begin{pmatrix} I_{n/2} & D_{n/2} \\ I_{n/2} & -D_{n/2} \end{pmatrix}
  = (H \otimes I_{n/2})
    \begin{pmatrix} I_{n/2} & 0 \\ 0 & D_{n/2} \end{pmatrix},
  \label{eq:butterfly-id}
\end{equation}
with $H = \bigl(\begin{smallmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$ the unnormalized Hadamard matrix. Substituting into~\cref{eq:fft} gives
\begin{equation}
  F_n \;=\; \bigl( H \otimes I_{n/2} \bigr) \,
            \begin{pmatrix} I_{n/2} & 0 \\ 0 & D_{n/2} \end{pmatrix} \,
            \bigl( I_2 \otimes F_{n/2} \bigr).
  \label{eq:butterfly}
\end{equation}
The rightmost factor applies $F_{n/2}$ to the sub-register $(q_1, \ldots, q_{k-1})$; the middle factor is a diagonal twiddle conditioned on $q_0$; the leftmost factor is a Hadamard acting on $q_0$ alone.

\emph{Decompose} (\cref{fig:cooley_tukey_to_qft}c). The conditional twiddle in the middle of~\cref{eq:butterfly} factors into two-qubit controlled-phase gates.
Using $D_n = \diag(1, \omega^{n/2}) \otimes \diag(1, \omega^{n/4}) \otimes \cdots \otimes \diag(1, \omega)$,
\begin{equation}
  \begin{pmatrix} I_{n/2} & 0 \\ 0 & D_{n/2} \end{pmatrix}
  \;=\; \prod_{j=1}^{k-1} \mathrm{CP}_{0,j}\bigl(M_j\bigr),
  \label{eq:cphase}
\end{equation}
with $M_j = \diag(1, 1, 1, e^{i\pi/2^{j-1}})$, where $\mathrm{CP}_{0,j}(M_j)$ applies $M_j$ to the qubit pair $(q_0, q_j)$.
The conditional twiddle has become a chain of $k - 1$ two-qubit diagonal gates.

\emph{Relax} (\cref{fig:cooley_tukey_to_qft}d). Recursing on $F_{n/2}$ replays the recursion-and-decomposition pair on the sub-register $(q_1, \ldots, q_{k-1})$, and so on down to $F_1 = H$ on the final qubit.
The complete network has $k$ Hadamards (one per qubit) and $k(k-1)/2$ controlled-phase gates (one per ordered qubit pair); contracting it costs $O(N \log N)$ and reproduces the classical FFT.
This fixed circuit is the standard Quantum Fourier Transform.
To obtain a \emph{parametric} circuit we relax each gate into a free element of its unitary manifold: every Hadamard becomes an unconstrained element of $U(2)$ (four real parameters), and every $M_j$ becomes an unconstrained element of $\diag(1, 1, 1, e^{i\phi_j}) \in U(1)^4$ (one real parameter).
The resulting circuit has $4k + k(k-1)/2 = O(\log^2 N)$ real parameters in the image side $N = 2^k$, contains the classical DFT at the QFT parameter point, and is unitary at every parameter setting by construction.

\subsection{Two-dimensional transforms and parameter count}

For a 2D image of size $2^m \times 2^n$, the separable 2D DFT is $F_m \otimes F_n$.
We apply the 1D parametric circuit independently to the $m$-qubit row register and the $n$-qubit column register, giving $m + n$ Hadamards and $m(m-1)/2 + n(n-1)/2$ controlled-phase gates across both dimensions.
The parameter count remains $O(\log^2 N)$ in the image side $N = 2^m = 2^n$, and the circuit still initializes exactly at the classical 2D DFT.
\Cref{sec:circuits} introduces three variants of this separable construction that differ only in their gate connectivity: an Entangled QFT that couples corresponding row and column qubits, a TEBD-style ring, and a MERA-inspired hierarchical topology.

\section{Riemannian optimization details}
\label{app:riemannian}

\subsection{Cayley retraction derivation}
\label{app:cayley}

For a unitary matrix $U \in U(2)$ and tangent vector $\xi = US$ where $S^\dagger = -S$, the Cayley retraction~\cite{wen2013feasible, li2020efficient, tagare2011notes} maps:
\begin{align}
  W &= \xi U^\dagger = USU^\dagger, \\
  W &\leftarrow \frac{W - W^\dagger}{2} \quad \text{(project to } \mathfrak{u}(2)\text{)}, \\
  R_U(\alpha \xi) &= \left(I - \frac{\alpha}{2}W\right)^{-1}\left(I + \frac{\alpha}{2}W\right)U.
\end{align}

Since $W$ is skew-Hermitian, $(I - \frac{\alpha}{2}W)^{-1}(I + \frac{\alpha}{2}W)$ is unitary for any $\alpha \in \RR$, as can be verified by checking that the product with its adjoint equals the identity.

For $2 \times 2$ matrices, the inverse admits a closed form via the adjugate:
\begin{equation}
  (I - \frac{\alpha}{2}W)^{-1} = \frac{1}{\det(I - \frac{\alpha}{2}W)} \operatorname{adj}(I - \frac{\alpha}{2}W).
\end{equation}

\subsection{Parallel transport for Riemannian Adam}
\label{app:parallel_transport}

For $U(2)$, the projection-based transport from $U_t$ to $U_{t+1}$ is:
\begin{equation}
  \Gamma_{U_t \to U_{t+1}}(m_t) = U_{t+1} \cdot \skew(U_{t+1}^\dagger m_t).
\end{equation}

For $U(1)^4$, the transport is the tangent projection at the new point:
\begin{equation}
  \Gamma_{\mathbf{z}_t \to \mathbf{z}_{t+1}}(m_t) = i \cdot \operatorname{Im}(\bar{\mathbf{z}}_{t+1} \circ m_t) \circ \mathbf{z}_{t+1}.
\end{equation}

Both are exact to first order in the step size.

\subsection{Riemannian gradient descent with Armijo line search}
\label{app:armijo_gd}

In addition to Riemannian Adam (\cref{sec:riemannian}), the framework implements a vanilla Riemannian gradient descent variant with Armijo backtracking, used for early development and as a baseline check.
Updates take the form $x_{t+1} = R_{x_t}(-\eta \cdot \grad f(x_t))$, where $\eta$ is determined by Armijo backtracking: starting from $\alpha_0$, contract $\alpha \leftarrow \tau \alpha$ (with $\tau = 0.5$) until the sufficient-decrease condition is satisfied:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
  \LL\big(R_{x_t}&(-\alpha\, \grad f(x_t))\big) \\
  &\leq \LL(x_t) - c\, \alpha\, \|\grad f(x_t)\|^2,
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
with $c = 10^{-4}$ and $\alpha_0 = 0.01$, and at most ten contraction steps per iteration.
This variant is not used to produce the rate-distortion numbers reported in the main text.

\subsection{Alternative loss functions}
\label{app:alt_losses}

Two natural alternatives to the MSE loss in~\cref{eq:mse_loss} were considered during development.
The $\ell_1$ norm $\LL_{L_1} = \sum_{i,j} |\TT(\mathbf{x})_{i,j}|$ is the tightest convex relaxation of $\ell_0$ and promotes coefficient sparsity directly on the transformed image.
The $\ell_2$ norm $\LL_{L_2} = \sum_{i,j} |\TT(\mathbf{x})_{i,j}|^2$ is the standard energy-concentration objective.
Both are proxies for the downstream reconstruction quality measured by MSE: neither operates on the post-truncation reconstruction, so gradients do not flow through the truncation layer.
Preliminary experiments showed that the MSE objective trains more stably and yields better rate-distortion than either proxy, so all reported results use~\cref{eq:mse_loss}.

\subsection{Batched einsum evaluation}
\label{app:einsum}

The forward transform is implemented as an Einstein summation (einsum) contraction of the image tensor with the gate tensors, following the differentiable tensor-network tradition~\cite{liao2019differentiable}.
The contraction order is pre-optimized using a tree-search algorithm (TreeSA) that minimizes the total operation count.
For batched training, a batch index $\beta$ is appended to the image input/output indices:
\begin{equation}
  Y_{q'_1, \ldots, q'_{m+n}, \beta} = \sum_{q_1, \ldots, q_{m+n}} \left(\prod_j G^j_{\cdots}\right) X_{q_1, \ldots, q_{m+n}, \beta}.
\end{equation}
The gate tensors are shared across the batch, reducing GPU kernel launch overhead from $O(B)$ to $O(1)$.

\section{Circuit topology details}
\label{app:alt_topologies}

This appendix gives the gate-level construction of the two connectivity foils (TEBD and MERA) summarized in~\cref{sec:alt_topologies}, and concludes with a side-by-side gallery of the six trained-basis topologies at the Quick Draw geometry $m = n = 5$.

\subsection{TEBD basis}

Time-Evolving Block Decimation~\cite{vidal2004tebd} uses a \emph{ring topology} of nearest-neighbor controlled-phase gates $T_k = \diag(1, 1, 1, e^{i\phi_k})$ preceded by Hadamard gates on each qubit, with wrap-around closing each register's ring independently.
The row and column rings have $n$ phase gates each ($2n$ learnable phases total) and the circuit evaluates in $O(n)$ gate operations per dimension, the cheapest of the four topologies at inference.
The parameter manifold is $\MM_{\text{TEBD}} = \prod_{k=1}^{2n} U(1)^4$.
TEBD's purpose in our comparison is to isolate connectivity from parameter count: it has twice as many phase parameters as the Entangled QFT's entanglement gates but arranges them with strictly local connectivity (\cref{fig:alt_topologies}, left).

\subsection{MERA-inspired basis}

Multi-scale Entanglement Renormalization~\cite{vidal2007mera} uses hierarchical connectivity with disentanglers and isometries at increasing spatial scales.
Our MERA-inspired basis borrows the connectivity pattern but parameterizes each gate as $\diag(1, 1, 1, e^{i\phi})$ for consistency with the other bases and to preserve invertibility.
For $n = 2^k$ qubits, the circuit has $\log_2 n$ layers with stride $2^{l-1}$ in layer $l$, totalling $2(n-1)$ gates per dimension and $O(\log n)$ depth.
The hierarchical connectivity captures multi-scale features: layer~1 mixes nearest neighbors, deeper layers mix distant qubits.
MERA's purpose in our comparison is to test whether \emph{multi-scale} connectivity (a weaker form of all-to-all) suffices to match the Entangled QFT; \cref{sec:topology_div2k} shows that it does not (\cref{fig:alt_topologies}, right).

\section{Block-size sweep}\label{app:block_size_sweep}

We sweep the inner block size $b$ across $\{2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64, 128\}$ on DIV2K and $\{2, 4, 8, 16\}$ on Quick Draw, at four retention ratios $\rho \in \{0.05, 0.10, 0.15, 0.20\}$, training each block-wrapped trained-basis cell at the same 1{,}008-step preset used for the headline tables (\cref{sec:setup}).
\Cref{fig:appendix_block_sweep_div2k,fig:appendix_block_sweep_quickdraw} render the multi-$\rho$ PSNR curves on a 2$\times$3 grid: top row classical (BlockFFT, BlockDCT, BlockBD-PCA), bottom row trained block-wrapped (Blocked QFT, RichBasis, RealRichBasis). Each diamond marks a family's peak PSNR per $\rho$, with the value annotated.
Three observations support the discussion in \cref{sec:blocksize}.
First, on DIV2K every dataset-fitted family domes at $b = 8$ at the headline $\rho = 0.20$, with the trained-basis peak reproducing the existing $\sim$33.7~dB headline.
Second, the peak position migrates with $\rho$: at $\rho \le 0.10$ the classical bases prefer $b = 16$ and the trained bases $b = 32$, because the limited coefficient budget pays best across a wider window dominated by a few low-frequency modes.
Third, on Quick Draw the BlockDCT and BlockFFT families \emph{decrease monotonically} with $b$ at high $\rho$, while the dataset-fitted bases (BlockBD-PCA, RealRichBasis) prefer $b = 4$; the optimal block size is therefore content-dependent and rate-dependent in different ways on the two datasets.
The within-family choice of inner topology contributes less than $2$~dB at the (family $\times \rho$)-best $b$ on either dataset, much smaller than the cross-$b$ spread at fixed $\rho$.

\paragraph{Implementation note.}
RealRichBasis and RichBasis at $b = 32$ on DIV2K (\ie $\text{inner}\_m = \text{inner}\_n = 5$, ten qubits per inner block) emit $2(m + m^2) = 60$ fresh wire labels in their einsum representation, exceeding the 52-character \texttt{a-zA-Z} pool of the original \texttt{pdft} circuit builder. Eliminating the pool required replacing the single-string contraction with a stepped \texttt{jnp.tensordot} path that applies one gate per call and recycles labels across steps; the external API and Hadamard-first tensor ordering are preserved, so saved checkpoints remain compatible (\texttt{pdft} pull request~15).

\section{Content-adaptive block size, HEVC-style}\label{app:adaptive_block}

The \cref{app:block_size_sweep} sweep establishes that the optimal $b$ depends on both content statistics and operating $\rho$; no single fixed $(basis, b)$ Pareto-dominates across content $\times$ rate.
This is the empirical motivation for HEVC's content-adaptive transform-unit selection.
We evaluate the per-image oracle form of that adaptivity: for each test image, choose the candidate $b \in \{4, 8, 16, 32\}$ (DIV2K) or $\{2, 4, 8, 16\}$ (Quick Draw) that minimises whole-image MSE at $\rho = 0.20$.
The choice is made on the test image itself, so the reported number is an upper bound on practically deployable per-image adaptivity (a real encoder would have to encode the choice in side information at a small rate cost).

We compare three families: BlockDCT (the HEVC-style classical baseline), BlockBD-PCA (the dataset-fitted classical), and the headline trained RealRichBasis. \Cref{fig:appendix_adaptive_per_image} summarizes the result.

\begin{figure*}[!htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{adaptive/adaptive_per_image.pdf}
  \caption{Per-image oracle adaptive block-size selection at $\rho = 0.20$, $n = 50$ test images. Left: DIV2K-8q. Right: Quick Draw, with a broken y-axis to accommodate the BlockDCT $b = 2$ outlier (54.9~dB at near-blank tiles, see \cref{app:block_size_sweep}). For each family, the lighter grey bar is the best fixed-$b$ over the test set; the coloured bar is the per-image oracle. The annotation above each adaptive bar is the gain in dB; the $b{=}$ label inside the fixed bar is the chosen fixed-best block size.}
  \label{fig:appendix_adaptive_per_image}

Three observations follow.
First, the gains are uniformly small: $\le 0.11$~dB across all six (dataset $\times$ family) cells. Per-image adaptivity is a bounded headroom, not a new regime.
Second, the dataset-fitted classical (BlockBD-PCA) shows the \emph{largest} adaptivity gain on both datasets (+0.11 and +0.07~dB); the data-fitted KLT can adapt at evaluation time without retraining, so heterogeneous test images benefit from per-image $b$ selection.
Third, the trained RealRichBasis on DIV2K has the \emph{smallest} gain (+0.008~dB): 45 of the 50 test images select the same $b = 8$.
The trained basis has implicitly absorbed per-image adaptivity through training, leaving little residual headroom for an oracle to extract.
This is consistent with the basic-variant emergent block factorization of \cref{sec:emergence}: training pushes the basis toward the dataset's typical content scale.

\paragraph{Per-CTU adaptive (out of scope).}
HEVC's transform-unit RDO operates intra-image, choosing $b$ per coding tree unit rather than per whole image. The per-CTU oracle on DIV2K with classical inner transforms is a natural follow-up; it requires a different truncation protocol (per-CTU uniform top-$k$, not the global top-$k$ used throughout this paper) and is left to future work alongside the full rate-distortion comparison against entropy-coded codecs (HEVC HM/x265, JPEG-XL, learned codecs of~\cite{balle2017end}).
